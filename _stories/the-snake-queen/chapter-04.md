In a universe where nothing made sense, a sudden spark of understanding paved the way for Raajimera to claim the gifts she had been promised. Each grain of sand she lifted felt like a small step closer to perfection. 

Feng turned out to be a good friend and a surprisingly useful ally. When nobody was looking, they would slip out to practice together. 

Raajimera remained confused, but just a glance at her companion's face was enough to ground her. She was determined to become a powerful demigoddess. 

The woman was not sure what life she had left behind, but the vision of her future filled her with motivation. She was going to repay the village of kind people with interest for the home they had given her. One day, she would protect them all.

The sun was setting after a really long day for the newly initiated demigoddess. Raajimera and Feng were walking back home after a successful training session.

The woman still felt a bit dizzy from handling the spirit animal magic. It wasn't something anyone could tamper with, and, despite everything, occasionally she was close to having second thoughts about whether Raa had made a good choice in trusting her.

Raajimera just wanted to lie down. The entire day filled with practice really wore her out. She was in the middle of fantasizing about the embrace of her bed when someone stopped her in her tracks. 

The woman looked up and saw the handsome athlete, the very same one beloved by the entire village.

"Well, hello there," he said as he blocked the way with his arm dauntingly.

She glanced at him and narrowed her eyes. The man seemed taller than she was. His muscles, while impressive, were also quite intimidating. Raajimera tried to act unaffected.

"Oh, did I scare you?" he asked. "I'm sorry. You've been with us for several days now, but you didn't seem to speak with anyone, so I thought I would break the ice. You understand me, right?"

Raajimera nodded quietly, unsure what her next move should be.

"May I ask what your name is?" the man said and moved closer to her.

She looked at his face, then put her hand in the pocket of her dress to make sure that Feng was still with her. She needed all the emotional support she could get. The woman felt like it was about time to make peace with the fact that she was never going back to who she once was. One thing was sure: if Feng was there with her, everything would be alright, and if this little critter was good, perhaps Raa was too?

The woman exhaled sharply and said, "It's Raajimera." She smiled softly, not without a grain of sadness. "That, at least, is what was chosen for me."

The man looked at her and said, "I will be honest, I thought you were mute, but then I saw you speak some strange words to a wild critter, and I figured that maybe you didn't speak our language." He smiled. "I am pleased to learn that both of my presumptions were incorrect," he said, scratching his chin. "Oh, where are my manners? I'm Seyed."

Raajimera looked him in the eyes and said, after a brief moment of hesitation, "Nice to meet you, Seyed." It felt strange for her to finally speak with somebody who had limbs for once. 

"Likewise," he replied with excitement in his voice. "My people will be thrilled to finally talk to you!"

The woman crossed her arms and opposed, "I don't think I'm ready to speak with them just yet. I hope you don't mind."

"Of course, there's no pressure." He smiled once more and asked, "So, is it true that you are the Snake Queen?"

Raajimera scratched her head. "Would you believe me if I told you that I don't know?" she responded to a question with another question. "The last thing I remember from the start of my journey was a giant snake in the sky, and then before I knew it, I was here speaking with critters."

He nodded. "I understand. So you really don't remember anything from your life before that?"

"No, my brain was corroded like a rock in a river," she said in what had been intended to be a humorous response. It sounded a little bitter, though.

Seyed laughed, although she wasn't certain if it was genuine. 

"It's a great honor to be chosen by Twilight Mother," he stated. "Would you like to see our shrine? I think you would love it."

The woman agreed, and Seyed led the way. They went through the whole village until they reached a giant rock. Right in front of it was a statue of a snake with its mouth open. It looked just like Raa. Raajimera moved close to it and placed her hand on its cheek. 

"I know you brought me here, Mother," she said in the language of snakes. It was an intimate moment between her and the divine being who bestowed its gift upon her. "I think I know why. I will protect your followers if this is your will." The woman put her forehead against the statue and closed her eyes. 

The mark on her head started glowing slightly, and she could see an image in the darkness. It was Raa, showing her how to use an ability she never heard of. 

"I understand," she said as she opened her eyes.

She kissed the cheek of the statue and thanked the snake goddess.

Raajimera moved to the table with offerings that stood right next to the shrine. There was an abundance of lit candles and gifts for Raa. The woman lifted her hand theatrically above the flame. She snapped her finger and caused a small amount of sand to dance with it above the candle. They spun around harmoniously until they merged into what looked like small glass lotuses. She collected them and carefully placed them next to real flowers.

Suddenly, she remembered that she wasn't alone. She turned around and saw Seyed gawking at her with his eyes wide open. He couldn't believe what he just saw.

"I'm sorry, I was starstruck," she said, trying to explain herself.

Seyed shook his head. "No, I apologize; I've never seen anything quite like this before." He hesitated, then continued, "It proves that you **truly** are the Snake Queen. We are so blessed to have you here in this time of need."

He moved a bit closer to her, as if he were trying to check if he could kiss her.

She wanted to say something, but suddenly she sensed that Feng, who was still inside her pocket, was attempting to grab her attention. The woman had no idea what was going on, but she felt like it was urgent. 

Raajimera backed away and said, "Actually, I am sorry. I have to return to my room now."

"Already?" Seyed asked. He sounded surprised. "I hoped I could show you more things and get to know each other better, perhaps."

"I really need to take care of something right now, maybe some other day. Thank you for bringing me here." Raajimera left the place, rushing. 

Seyed turned to the statue and waited until he could no longer hear the woman's footsteps. He looked at the statue of Raa and took one of the glass flowers from the table. He examined it closely, turning it in his fingers, observing how the light moves through them. Then he smiled to himself and slipped it into his pocket.

"I hope you won't need **all** of these, Twilight Mother," he said to himself and smirked. 

The air suddenly turned cold and sharp in a way that was unnatural for this climate. It was not sure whether it was caused by the approaching night or the goddess's disappointment in the petty altar thief who worshiped her. 

Raajimera's steps were swift. She quickly climbed the stairs of the inn where she lived, closed the door loudly, and took a seat on her bed. The woman took her snake pal out of her pocket.

The animal seemed agitated.

"Did I do something wrong?" she asked with concern in her voice.

Feng looked at her and exclaimed, "You? No!"

The animal was restless. "When you went to the shrine, something changed in Raa's energy; it's concerning," Feng finally explained.

"Do you think she is disappointed with me because I communed with her in front of a village person?" she asked worriedly. 

"No, I don't think it's that," Feng responded. The animal seemed puzzled. "I can't quite put my tail tip on what is wrong."

"Why do you think this is a problem?" Raajimera asked. "She taught me a new skill, and I managed to follow her instructions on my first try!"

"This has nothing to do with you, I think—"

Some ruckus outside interrupted their conversation. They looked at each other and approached the round window of Raajimera's room. 

"It seems like the whole village gathered around the well for some reason," she said. "This doesn't look good; do you think I should go there and see what happened?"

Feng nodded. "I think it would be a good idea," it said. "Bring me with you."

Raajimera put her snake companion in her hidden pocket and rushed outside. She didn't dare to come too close, so she just observed the village people from afar. 

The village chieftain noticed her in one way or another and decided to start a conversation. 

"We seem to be experiencing a water shortage right now," he said gloomily. "The last days have been pretty harsh, but I would have sworn the situation was still manageable about an hour ago." The chieftain sighed. "We thought that bringing offerings to the goddess would bring us her favor, but something seems to have worsened the situation. Do you think you could help us, herald of Raa?"

Feng slithered out of her pocket and crawled up to her shoulder. "You could draw the water from the ground and bring it to the surface," the reptile hissed quietly into her ear.

Raajimera lowered her voice. "How can I do that?" she asked. "I've just learned how to bend sand; I have no idea what you expect me to do."

"Roughly the same way. Water lines just look like glowing threads when you look at them with your third eye," the animal explained. "Seek, and you shall find them."

"I really hope you are right, and they won't kill me for being a fake deity," she said. The woman shouted, "Step back." 

Raajimera felt awkward as her loud voice dragged all the attention. She wished she could back down and retreat to the safety of silence, but it was too late now.

The crowd gasped and dispersed. All eyes were on her; she felt the pressure, and the regret she felt was overwhelming. It was difficult to focus on the task at hand with all the people staring at her in awe. 

"Hey," Feng said and gently touched her cheek with its tail tip. "Close your eyes and ground yourself. Try to feel the surrounding air, hear the insects playing their songs in the background, and notice the texture of the sand under your feet."

Raajimera closed her eyes. She couldn't see the village anymore; she stopped hearing and feeling. In that one second, there was only her and the infinite abyss of the ethereal plane. 

The woman lifted her hands and began doing calm moves resembling gentle martial arts. 

Among the darkness, her third eye saw a clone of herself made of shiny water. She could almost touch it if only she moved a little closer…

_Who are you_? She asked in her thoughts.

_I am a part of you_, the clone responded. _I have always been. You are fragmented and weakened. Only when you unlock every part of you will you become powerful._ 

_Do you know who I was before?_ She asked again.

The clone shook its head. _Forget about the past,_ it said. _You are a perfect blend of flesh and celestial magic. The gift of Raa is a powerful one, but also one given in parts. Just see what happens when you collect more pieces…_ The clone reached out to her and grabbed her hand.

A sudden flash of light blinded Raajimera, and she lost her balance. She opened her eyes and saw water sprouting out of the well like a geyser. The villagers cheered and began to collect it in whatever they could. 

The woman was still confused from her experience and couldn't understand what was going on. She looked up and felt like, for a split second, she could see the face of Raa in the darkening sky. 

"Mother…" she mumbled.

Suddenly, her view of the sky was obscured by Seyed's face. His hair, now wet, made his head appear smaller in proportion to the rest of his body.

The man gave her his hand, attempting to help her get up from the ground. She grabbed it, but something drew her attention. His skin seemed so soft, yet it was so coarse to the touch. It felt as confusing as walking on a carpet made of gravel. 

"Marvelous! We knew you would do it!" Seyed said as he clapped his hands. 

"Huh?" Raajimera looked around. Nobody paid even the slightest attention to her; all of these people were too busy collecting water. Her eyes returned to Seyed as she said, "What do you mean by 'we'?"

"Oh, I already told them that I made you speak," he answered as if that were obvious. "I spared them no detail of what happened at the shrine of Raa as well."

Raajimera felt slightly betrayed. She raised her eyebrow. "**You** made me?" she asked, for she couldn't believe what she heard. After so many days of struggling to accept her current situation, someone had just taken the credit like that? 

The woman wanted to argue with him, but he just turned around and spoke to his people. "You had your proof! The prophecy unveils before your very eyes!" Seyed threw his hands up and continued, "We will make the Emperor answer for what he has done to us and this country; we will make the Empire fear us!"

The village people cheered briefly and returned to their tasks. At that moment, Raajimera felt incredibly selfish by making it about herself. She remembered that the village was in danger, and she must keep going on until she can protect them all from the evil emperor. The woman lowered her head. 

Seyed left her standing there in the mud; he went off to help his peers. 

Raajimera's eyes followed him—suddenly something came to her attention. She lowered her voice and spoke to Feng. "Now it occurred to me that I never asked them how their prophecy ends," she said.

"What do you mean?" the animal asked, raising its head to look at her.

"They said that their village is supposed to be saved by the Snake Queen, but they didn't tell me what will happen afterward," she explained. 

"You could try asking around the village," Feng pointed out. "It makes me wonder where they got it from."

"Huh?" Raajimera was confused. "I thought they heard it from Raa?"

Feng shook its head. "Raa has nothing to do with it. As far as I know, neither do the remaining spirit animals. This is rather peculiar, if you ask me."

"But if not Raa," she wondered, "then who told them about the prophecy?" Raajimera scratched her head and continued, "It sounded too specific, but the fact that I am here means that it's real."

Feng moved its tail in a way that made it seem as if it were shrugging. 

Raajimera looked around and noticed Aya and the village women trying to carry jugs of water. She approached them and asked if they needed help.

"Not really; you already helped us enough," Aya said, leaving her there alone.

Perhaps she was simply overthinking everything, but something didn't sit right with Raajimera. She couldn't stop the rush of thoughts, so she decided to do something to distract herself from it all.

The woman headed back towards the tavern, but as she approached the door, she heard a group of villagers talking about something right behind the building. Their voices were hushed, so she couldn't really understand what their conversation was about. She thought she heard something about the empire, although she was not entirely sure.

Raajimera entered the tavern and brought a pot with her. 

There was an old terracotta bathtub in the room next to her. She intended to collect some fresh water to clean herself with. 

After many rounds back and forth, she had finally managed to fill the bathtub. It was then that she realized that the water was too cold to bathe in. Raajimera sat on a small stool next to it with a defeated expression.

Suddenly, Feng emerged from hiding. "What is it?" it asked.

"I was going to take a bath, but then I realized that the water was too cold," she explained. "Do you think I could go to the kitchen and ask the workers to boil some water for me?"

The snake gazed intently at her. 

"What?" Raajimera seemed clueless. The animal quietly kept observing her, so she said, "Don't say I can do this myself as well."

Feng nodded. "You can. It's great to be a god, isn't it?" The snake asked.

Raajimera sighed. "I should have figured this out myself," she said as she rolled her eyes. "Guide me."

Feng hissed, "This is very similar to what you did before, only this time you have to visualize a small flame in your chest."

"Is that all?" she asked impatiently.

Feng slithered closer to the brown bathtub. "No," it said, "this is merely the first step. Now put both your hands on your heart and visualize extracting the flame and putting it on your palm."

Feng gave her a nod of approval. "Now bind yourself to the water the same way as you did at the well. Your inner flame should connect to it," the animal explained.

The woman gracefully moved her hands, and after several minutes, the water was pleasantly warm, only to reach the boiling point within several seconds.

Raajimera squeaked.

"No, that's too much!" Feng shouted, "Revert it before you kill someone with this overgrown, boiling flowerpot falling from the ceiling!"

"I don't know how!" Raajimera panicked.

"You must have used a flame that was too large," Feng explained. "Just imagine extracting some of it from the water. It should stabilize the temperature."

Eventually, the temperature dropped. Now, the water was serene and pleasantly warm.

"See, you've done it!" Feng sounded excited. "You are a natural! I mean, as natural as a person who got their powers from a deity can be. " If snakes could smile, this one would definitely do it right now.

Raajimera began undressing. Feng looked at her and asked, "It may sound strange, but… may I bathe with you? I'm about to molt, and my skin feels really dry."

The woman placed her animal companion by her side and sat down in the pleasantly warm water. 

Feng hissed, "Ahh, yesssss." The snake was beaming with joy. 

Raajimera laughed briefly, and quickly her facial expression turned serious. "I also have a question for you," she said.

The animal looked at her with its beady eyes while floating on the surface of the water like a toy. 

The woman scratched her arm awkwardly. "When I first connected with the underground river, I saw a clone of myself made of water. It spoke to me. Do you think it's normal?"

"You're a demigoddess with powers from the sky, speaking with feral snakes; what do you consider normal?" Feng asked. 

"I don't know," Raajimera mumbled and looked away. "The clone said that the gift I got is given in parts, and I will only be powerful once I claim them all."

"Hmmm," Feng hissed. "Don't think too much about it; I think it was just Raa trying to motivate you to hone your skills."

Raajimera nodded. She almost fell asleep in the bathtub. 

The woman decided to just prepare herself for sleeping. She went out of the tub, dried herself with a piece of cloth, and returned to her room. 

Raajimera wrapped herself with the colorful blanket from her bed and approached the window. 

The village people were already in their homes; the well was abandoned, surrounded by mud and empty vessels. Raajimera looked up and saw a dark night sky filled with thousands of sparkling stars shining like a myriad of little flames. 

"It's so beautiful; would you look at it, Feng?" She placed the animal on her palm and lifted it up so that it could also witness the beauty of the night sky.

The snake lifted its little snout and asked, "Can you see that one big star? The brightest one?" Raajimera responded with a nod, so Feng continued, "It is where Twilight Mother is when she isn't called. Whenever I feel scared or worried, I look at this star and know that she's with me. It makes difficult times easier to bear."

Raajimera smiled and gazed upon the star.

"I still don't know what your plans for me are, but… thank you, Raa, for putting such a wonderful friend in my way." She rubbed her cheek against Feng's. They both seemed so happy.

_Maybe it was because she wanted to believe._

_Perhaps it was because she didn't know how to listen._

_But that night, the universe sang quietly._

_Perhaps this isn't the only thing put in her way by fate…_
