The chieftain has been downcast lately. Nobody was sure whether it was caused by the possibility of an impending imperial attack or if there was something different he was not telling them. Regardless, he was clearly lost in thought. 

This fact gave Raajimera a window of opportunity to finally get some answers to her questions about the prophecy. She had been asking about it for quite a while now, but nobody seemed to know the answer she needed. It was about time she found them on her own.

That morning, as on every other day since Raajimera appeared in the settlement, the chieftain struggled to stay asleep. He woke up blinded by the morning sun rays. The man looked like a bear with a splinter in its foot. The chieftain quickly got dressed and left his house. 

Raajimera observed him from her hiding spot. If the answers to the questions that bothered her were somewhere, it must have been in his house. She scouted it multiple times over the days. 

The woman's eyes followed the chieftain until he disappeared from her sights. Raajimera intended to use the most recent trick that Feng taught her. She created an illusion resembling the chieftain and stepped through it to wear it as a disguise. It looked very convincing; she was sure it would fool just about anyone. 

He locked his door, but a mere lock was not enough to stop a freshly baked demigoddess. Raajimera approached the door and forced it open with her celestial powers. She gained the entrance.

The stench of dusty air pierced her nostrils. It seemed that this man was clearly unbothered by that, but to Raajimera it was almost unbearable. She hoped Raa would take away her sense of smell as well. Human body functions were the weakest part of being a demigod, in her opinion.

The room was cluttered and big, but she had a faint idea of what she should check. She approached one of the bookshelves and attempted to find some sort of a tome that would have the prophecy written down. 

Raajimera found a couple of useless books, several scrolls, and random items, but nothing seemed to help. She suddenly noticed a thick book with no title on it. The woman grabbed it and noticed that there was a medium-sized wooden box behind it. She took it and saw a symbol of a snake painted on top of it. 

Before she managed to open it and look inside, the door suddenly opened, and she jumped and turned around, pushing the box back into the bookshelf. 

Seyed stood in the doorframe, measuring the illusion of the chieftain suspiciously with his eyes.

"Why are you still here?" he asked. "Didn't you say you would leave this morning?"

Luckily he didn't see through the spell. She was glad he didn't, but he also lost all of his charm; his voice sounded rude and harsh.

"Oh, yes, I was about to, but then I remembered I forgot something…" she lied and attempted to move close to the exit. 

"On a bookshelf? Are you going to read the traveling merchant a goodnight story?" Seyed's voice rang with disbelief. "I thought you only kept books as a decoration."

"Oh, no. It was something else…and I already got it! See you." Raajimera left quickly without breaking her disguise. That was close, too close. 

Seyed's eyes followed her as she disappeared from the building. He mysteriously brushed his chin and approached the bookshelf. The man grabbed the box and opened it to verify whether the contents were still intact. 

"It's strange that he would check if it's still here," he said to himself. "Doesn't he believe in me?" He shook his head and closed the box. 

Raajimera dropped her disguise and squeezed herself into a dark corner. 

_So the chieftain is meeting some wandering merchant, huh?_ She thought to herself. _Since he has the box, maybe he would tell me more about the prophecy. Either him or the merchant._

Feng slithered from behind the building and looked at her. "Oh no, I know this look. Don't tell me you've got another plan," it said. "Whatever it is, it's a bad idea!"

"What?" She looked down at her friend. "Why? I need to know more about the prophecy; it's about me after all."

"Because you are very likely to attract unwelcome attention. They may like you now, but who knows how they will react to your tricks?" Feng wondered. "I'm sure there is some other way of getting answers than doing this. HEY, ARE YOU LISTENING TO ME?" The snake raised its voice.

"No," Raajimera said and smiled widely as she put an illusion of Seyed on herself. "I think the chieftain might tell our friend what he won't tell me."

"Raajimera, please," the snake begged. "I saw your first attempt; you almost got caught! You don't know what they will do when they find out. Don't test them."

The woman scoffed. "I got it under control," she said. "I am a demigoddess, remember?"

"This sort of ego is what leads people straight into deals with Raa. Don't let it overwhelm you once more," Feng warned her.

Raajimera tried to hide her displeased expression. "I will be careful," she assured her friend, "but you can't stop me from doing this."

The snake watched her helplessly as she left. The animal decided to follow her from afar.

<br><br>

The chieftain was waiting on the outskirts of the village. He was restless and grumpy, looking at the horizon, clearly still waiting for the merchant. That was an opportunity for Raajimera to have a conversation with him. Alone. 

Not wasting any time, the demigoddess assumed the form of Seyed and approached the chieftain.

The man heard her before she got to speak. He looked at her and raised an eyebrow.

She stopped cluelessly and mustered some courage to speak. The voice of Seyed flowed from her lips, "I wanted to ask about the prophecy."

The chieftain frowned. "Again? I thought you said it was secured," he said. "I don't have time to talk about it over and over."

"I was thinking that maybe we should tell her more about it," Raajimera replied.

"Have you lost your mind, Seyed?" the chieftain asked. "That would complicate everything!"

Raajimera wanted to say something, but suddenly she saw the real Seyed approaching from the other side with the corner of her eye. She pointed at something in the sky and ran away when the chieftain looked away. 

The real Seyed looked at the chieftain suspiciously; the leader did the same.

"Is he still not here?" Seyed asked. "I thought he would be waiting for you since you decided to stay home for longer."

"What are you talking about?" The chieftain was confused. "I left home very early today. Why did you move? Weren't you on my other side just a second ago?"

Seyed's eyes swept the area quickly, in a way that reminded Raajimera less of a confused man and more of someone checking if they had been followed. His expression then settled back into his usual ease. He crossed his arms as if nothing had happened.

"What? Huh, never mind," he said. "I will wait for him with you."

The two men sat on tree stumps nearby and continued chatting.

Raajimera observed them from hiding, but she couldn't hear a word of what they were saying. She began to think she was terrible at planning. Even an animal had a better understanding of the situation, and it really bothered her.

Feng finally caught up with her. It coiled around her ankle and quietly waited as well. Raajimera really needed Feng's presence right now.

Hours were passing, and when the day was in its full bloom, they noticed a man approaching from the west. 

He was quite short, but he looked like a regular human. His silhouette reminded her of a plump, dark olive with a scarf on its head, bouncing on top of the caravan pulled by his two trusty donkeys. 

The merchant jumped to the ground and dusted off his sleeve. "I apologize for the delay," he said. "It's just the damn imperial soldiers everywhere. Anyway, I got the supplies you asked for."

The guest opened the door of his caravan and pulled various bags of goods out of it. In return, the chieftain presented him with a bag of gold. 

"So what's new on the road?" the chieftain asked as he inspected the bags he was given.

"I'm going to be honest with you, pal." The merchant took out a pipe from the pocket of his vest and filled it with tobacco. "It doesn't look good at all." 

"What do you mean?" Seyed asked.

The merchant started smoking. "The imperial patrols are getting more frequent with each day passing. If they find out that I'm shipping my wares to you, I will be done for." He drew his thumb across his neck to signify how dire the situation was. 

The men fell silent.

"Moreover," the merchant continued, "rumor has it that Zephyr was spotted sniffing around too."

"Incredible, so we have that shadow on our backs too," the chieftain moaned. "I will never know from under what rock he crawled out, but he always seems to find a way to ruin my plans. Do you think he knows about, you know…?"

"I sure as damnation hope he doesn't," the merchant said and exhaled some smoke. "You know him, though. That man seems to have a personal vendetta against you; if he is nearby, that probably means trouble."

Both the chieftain and Seyed seemed displeased about it. 

Raajimera had to know what was going on. She crawled out of her corner and approached the caravan. The woman had already dropped her disguise so they could see clearly who she was.

The stranger noticed her right away. "Who is she?" he inquired. "She looks absolutely stunning! Let me take a look at you!"

"Oh, isn't she?" Seyed said with so much admiration in his eyes that she almost forgot what his real face looked like this morning. She found it funny how different he was when he was sure it was her.

"I'm Raajimera," the woman said. 

"We found her at the entrance to the village a few weeks ago," Seyed explained briefly. 

"She initially didn't speak, but we noticed the mark on her forehead and assumed she was the fabled Snake Queen," the chieftain added.

The merchant glanced at her forehead. "Yes, it makes sense," he replied.

"She can do some sort of god magic and whatnot," Seyed explained without sugarcoating it. 

The lack of praise in his voice made Raajimera feel like the entire village was taking her miracles for granted. She did so much to prove to them that her faith in her was not misplaced, but she didn't know how to truly impress them. The woman really desired to have them acknowledge her. 

Perhaps that was why they wouldn't tell her about the prophecy? Perhaps if she did something spectacular, the ultimate miracle, if you will, they would finally acknowledge her full potential. Raajimera could already imagine the village treating her as their marvelous savior. 

The merchant finished smoking and put his pipe back in the pocket of his vest. "Would you like to purchase something?" he asked her. "I can give you some out-of-business discounts! I got some beautiful dresses and bracelets on the back of my caravan. Only ten pieces of silver per piece!"

"Silver?" Raajimera was confused. "I don't have any. How about these?" She pulled some of her crystals out of her pocket. 

The vendor examined them. "I'm not even sure what they are, but I'm pretty sure they don't sell for a lot." He wrinkled his nose.

"So I can't buy anything?" Raajimera asked.

"I am willing to offer you some aged footwear or worn-out pieces of cloth. Oh, you know what?" The man reached deep into his caravan's storage and pulled out a sack. "You are fond of flowers, right? Take this bag of old seeds. I mean, this place is basically a desert, but this is the best I can do for your…currency."

Raajimera really wanted to make her first purchase, so she took the bag without complaining. She handed him the glass flowers and looked into the sack.

"Growing them here would be a miracle, but I guess you are no stranger to those as the herald of Ra," he said and started packing.

An idea suddenly struck Raajimera. She already knew what her grand miracle was going to be. 

The merchant bid them farewell and hit the road. She decided to leave as well before they started asking uneasy questions. 

The woman swiftly walked across the village and sat on a bench. She put her hand in the sack and retrieved several seeds out of it. They looked pretty bad, but she was sure it wouldn't be impossible to work with them.

Feng suddenly appeared on her lap and put its little snout in the sack.

"What will you do with these?" it asked with its voice muffled. "Are you going to eat them?"

Raajimera shook her head and replied, "I've got to check something." She pulled the little reptile out of the sack and sat on the ground. 

The woman knelt and poked a hole in the dirt with her index finger. She checked if its position was right and then dropped a single seed into it. After it sat comfortably in the sandy soil, she buried it with both her hands and stood up. 

Feng looked at her carefully.

Raajimera stretched her hands forward and directed her celestial power towards the buried seed. This time it was much easier for her to attune to the hidden energies of nature. She became one with them. With a swing of her hand, the woman pulled some water out of the ground and directed it towards the seed. 

The ground burst abruptly, and a lively green sprout shot out of it.

"I did it!" Raajimera shouted excitedly. "I can't believe it worked!"

Feng's eyes followed her as she bounced with joy. The animal didn't understand what was going on. 

The woman noticed the confusion on the snake's face and decided to explain, "Imagine if I could plant all of these seeds. I could turn this place into a real oasis. It would be so…so…" Raajimera tried to think of the proper word, but nothing was coming to her.

"Difficult," Feng hinted. "You probably would not only have to repeat the process with every single seed in your sack but also borrow energy from the villagers. Not to mention the relentless sun, you would have to find a way to shield the plants."

"Why do you think I would need to include them?" Raajimera asked. "Shouldn't I have enough energy of my own?"

"You haven't reached the peak of your abilities yet," the snake responded. "After you fully ascend to the role of the avatar of Raa, perhaps, but for now you still have a way ahead of you."

"Hmmm." Raajimera cleared her throat grumpily. She supported her head on her fist, grabbed a stick, and began drawing in the dry soil. "I got an idea, though."

Feng looked at her doodles and tilted its head. "What is this?" it asked.

The woman drew a flower and a circle all around it. "What if I could create a dome above the village to protect the plants?" she asked. "I mean, if they had to lend me their energy one way or another, I believe this would be a good use of it." 

"Don't you think it would draw too much attention from afar?" Feng looked at her with its shiny eyes. 

"I was thinking I could make it see-through, but only from one side." Raajimera gestured vaguely. "That way it would conceal the village and protect its people."

"That is—" the snake said and paused to think of a proper word. "A good idea, actually." 

Raajimera smiled widely.

"I also think that we could use the plants as the power generator for the shield," Feng said and nodded to itself. "Their life energy would make it possible to maintain it indefinitely."

The woman rubbed her chin. "How would I achieve that?" she asked unsteadily.

Feng looked at her. "First you create an illusion, but direct it at multiple glass flowers rather than just one," the animal responded. "Then you distribute the stream of energy evenly between them and try to connect the points in your mind to create a grid on the ground. From that point on, the celestial power will do its magic."

Raajimera jumped excitedly, nearly knocking off the sack of seeds and spilling them on the ground. She caught the container at the last second. "Feng, I can't wait to begin my project!" Raajimera shouted eagerly. "This will be huge! I will finally pay them back for the care they have given me since I appeared in this village." 

The woman picked the snake up from the ground and nearly threw it into the sky out of excitement. 

"Careful!" Feng shouted. "I am tiny, you know?"

"I'm sorry!" Raajimera apologized and put the little critter's head to her cheek. "I'm just so happy to finally be able to create a good environment for the people who accepted me as one of them."

"I'm really proud of you," the animal said. "Several weeks ago you were just scared and confused, honestly thinking that Raa was your enemy. Look how well you adapted to your new role! Occasionally you end up doing silly things, but ultimately you are so compassionate—" 

The woman interrupted Feng's speech, "Imagine! I am finally going to be their hero, their savior…"

"Raajimera, you should have just stopped after '_one of them';_ it really doesn't do you any favors if you continue talking," the snake stated.

The woman was already in a different place emotionally. She grabbed her things and rushed toward an abandoned yard on the side of the village. 

For a moment, she had a thought so familiar that it almost frightened her. Raajimera liked the idea of being indispensable. She was eager to help, yet not out of her goodwill; she wanted to be powerful, to be owed.

Why did this thought feel like coming home to her? She had no memory of ever thinking this way. She tried to push this feeling down yet kept walking.

"No, my friend," she said under her breath, "I can't stop after anything. I've got the ultimate miracle to perform."

Feng couldn't shake the feeling that it didn't bode well for them, but the snake could do nothing besides helplessly observe his companion rushing out like a rock shot out of a slingshot. 

The animal carefully coiled itself around her wrist and hoped that she wouldn't give in to her dark side once more.
