The end of Raajimera's journey was within arms reach. She felt both unprepared and filled with a sense of purpose. For the first time ever, she knew what she was expected to do. Knowing, however, didn't make it easier to make peace with the task at hand.

Did she even have what it takes to kill a person? Even if taking this one life could save thousands of innocent prisoners, it still felt wrong to even consider that.

Raajimera knew that Raa operated on an entirely different level, and she didn't see it this way, but knowing and feeling are two separate things.

She just hoped her hesitation wouldn't make undeserving people pay for it.

Raajimera was sitting on the corner of the bed, still and unblinking, like a statue in front of the emperor's palace.

Feng climbed on her shoulder and stared intensively at her, hoping she would notice it. She didn't.

"Raajimera?" the snake finally said with concern in its voice.

The woman snapped out of it.

"What is it?" she asked.

"What's going on? We were beginning to worry about you," Feng said.

Raajimera made a confused face and glanced at Zephyr, who was also looking at her with concern.

"Oh, it's nothing," she said. "I'm just a little nervous."

More than a little.

Raajimera stretched and looked around. Zephyr's hideout was quite small, but she felt safer this way.

"I think we need to distract her with something," Feng stated, jolting towards Zephyr's belongings. "Let's see what is here…"

"Hey, leave it be!" the man shouted and followed the little snake.

The animal wrapped its tail around something and refused to let go.

"No! Let's open this box," it shouted as Zephyr tried to pull it away from his things.

"It's just a box of trinkets I found in the ground while traveling; there is nothing of value in there," he responded and continued to struggle with the animal's death grip.

"Let me see them!" Feng howled and coiled around the box even harder.

Zephyr tried to shake the animal off the container, but he finally gave up.

"Fine. Just release it and I will open it," he said.

"Can I trust you?" Feng squinted its eyes, displaying a suspicious expression.

"You're asking this now?" Zephyr answered with another question. "The time for inquiring about it was when you first met me. Now release the box."

"Fine," Feng responded with a pouty face. "You better open it now!"

The animal uncoiled and climbed on his shoulder, staring at the box in anticipation.

Zephyr sat next to Raajimera and opened the container. It was a wooden box with a white flower painted on top of the lid and a lot of random small trinkets inside.

Feng stared into it intensively.

"They are so shiny!" it finally shouted.

The animal jumped down and began admiring the trinkets. The box was full of mismatched buttons, missing brooches, hair accessories, and beads. Feng thought it also saw a pearl.

"This one reminds me of Raajimera," Zephyr said, and he lifted up a thick pale pink ribbon. It looked shiny and delicate.

"Why this one?" Feng asked, "I think this bead would fit her more."

The snake pointed at a medium-sized brown orb, which resembled amber.

"Because it's soft and rosy," Zephyr stated. "Why did you pick this bead?"

The animal looked at it again and said, "Because it's dark, but when you look at it from another angle, you can see the light passing through it and revealing a beautiful pattern."

"You know what?" the man asked. "I think you are right, but so am I. Let me try something."

Zephyr threaded the bead through the ribbon he picked. He grabbed Raajimera's hand and tied the trinket around her wrist like a bracelet.

The woman was surprised. She looked at her wrist and rotated it to get a better look.

"You made this for me… together…" she said, "…thank you."

She sounded grateful. It really meant a lot to her.

"See? I told you it would work," Feng exclaimed. "She is among the living again!"

"About time because I need some help," Zephyr said.

Raajimera looked at him slightly confused.

"My help?" she asked. "I thought you wanted to do everything on your own."

"And I thought you wanted to be included," he said. "Are you going to help me or not?"

"Of course," Raajimera answered and stood up. She helped Zephyr carry some bowls, plates, and other things outside.

The two went out. Zephyr seemed to have started a fire before she got up. He knelt in front of the campfire and began kneading some flour, oil, and a little bit of water into a lump of dough until it was fairly bouncy.

"What are you making?" she asked and sat next to him.

"Flatbread," he responded and put the dough into a bowl. "I put some rocks in the campfire to make them hot. It's enough to allow me to cook it."

Raajimera observed him.

The man started breaking off small pieces of the dough and began flattening them with his fingers.

"You are so resourceful," Raajimera noticed. "How did you learn so much?"

Zephyr smiled at her softly.

"Everything I can do, my father could do better," he said. "I was raised to be his successor as the chieftain of Duskvale. He believed it was important that I learn as much as possible ever since I was little."

The man removed one of the knives attached to his belt and used it to flip the flatbread.

"What is the name of the capital city?" Raajimera changed the topic. "I never hear anyone use it."

Zephyr looked at her and said, "It's Hazyan City. The emperor's bloodline was never a creative sort."

Raajimera nodded slowly.

"Give me a plate, please," Zephyr said and extended his hand.

She grabbed a wooden plate and passed it to him. The surface was clean, so he could just put the food on it without doing anything.

Zephyr lifted the small bread on the tip of his knife and dropped it on the plate.

"You may begin eating. Be careful, though. It's still hot and you might burn yourself," he advised.

It was already too late, as she was already mid-bite when he said that. Raajimera tried to hide her new injury.

The bread was quite plain, but preparing it on a campfire gave it a delicious smoky note.

Zephyr began cooking another piece of bread. He took a deep breath.

"I figured out that the emperor would be quite old at this point. How come he wasn't already married?" Raajimera asked.

"He was in the past. It's his fourth wedding," Zephyr answered.

"Fourth?!" Raajimera blurted out. "Are people in the empire polygamous? What is he doing with all these wives?"

"No...they aren't..." the man answered and shook his head. He placed another piece of bread on the plate and resumed cooking.

Zephyr took a bite of the bread.

"Raajimera, that's still too hot. How did you eat it so quickly?" he asked.

"I was hungry…" she responded and looked away.

The man put another piece of dough on the hot rocks.

"What happened to his previous wives?" Raajimera asked.

"Trust me, you don't want to know," he responded and sighed heavily.

Raajimera felt as if something heavy were weighing on her stomach. She looked at the ground.

"Why would they keep marrying him, then?" she mumbled.

Zephyr flipped the bread again and looked at her.

"They don't have any choice," he said. "If the emperor picks you, you've got to do it. The only way for women to escape his filthy tentacles is by joining a religious sisterhood."

Raajimera seemed saddened.

"What does he even look like?" she asked. "How would I recognize him?"

"Trust me, you can't miss him," he responded. "He's the only one who looks like a fiend from the depths of the underworld."

They finished eating. Before cleaning the area, Raajimera decided to quickly make some of her glass flowers. Zephyr quietly observed her conjuring them. No number of times seeing her use her celestial powers would ever make it any less impressive for him.

Raajimera collected the flowers and brought them into the hideout along with most of the things they had outside. Zephyr's gaze followed her as she left. He stayed outside a little longer to brew himself some hop-cone infusion. He knew that his hands would betray him if he didn't.

The man took this moment to spend some time alone. Raajimera and Feng were talking about something in the hideout. He sat down with his little cup of infusion and took a deep breath. There was something bothering him. He knew he would have to tell Raajimera about it sooner or later.

Zephyr sighed. It had been so long since he felt genuinely happy with another person. The thoughts of losing what he finally had filled him with sorrow.

The man finished his brew and extinguished the fire. He then stood above it for a short while with his hands on the back of his hips, just looking at the dying flame.

Zephyr walked back inside and looked at Raajimera. She seemed at peace. He wondered if she really was calm or if he finally learned to conceal her feelings.

"We will need to put some costumes on to enter the capital city safely," he said and put his cup and kettle away. "I will cover your mark again, but please, no surprises _this time_."

"I don't know what you're talking about," she responded and looked away.

Zephyr didn't react. He searched through his belongings and pulled two large packages out.

"Look at this," he said and lifted one of the contents up. It was a dark brown robe with golden ornaments. Two yellow stripes ran from the neck all the way to the bottom of the garment.

"Where did you get this?" Raajimera asked.

The outfit seemed expensive.

"I met a noble once," he said. "He thought himself dangerous. That man tried to track me down and get me killed. I had to teach him a lesson, so I robbed him and sent him running through the scorching desert sands in only his underwear."

"Weren't you worried he would tell someone what you looked like?" the woman inquired.

Zephyr shook his head.

"You see, I spread many lies about myself to poison the well," he answered. "I forced plenty of people to confess they were me. It would take more than a rich man's word to undo it."

Raajimera looked at him. At first she was just quietly processing what he just said.

"You are cruel sometimes," she summarized.

"Perhaps," Zephyr said and folded the robe, "but you got to be more than one thing to survive in this world."

She couldn't argue with that.

"The man who had the misfortune of facing me had this signet on him," Zephyr said and took out a golden ring from the hidden pocket of the piece of clothing he was holding.

It was heavy and shiny. Adorned with a symbol that looked like three circles connected together in the shape of a triangle.

"It's a symbol of status," he explained. "The guards are less likely to investigate the wearer because they could get in trouble for harassing the nobles."

"So you could be hiding in plain sight…" she said.

"Precisely," Zephyr confirmed. "I also got an outfit for you."

He put the brown robe on the bed and fetched another disguise. This time it was a dark teal dress with long sleeves adorned with golden thread at the bottom. It looked as bounteous as the previous garment.

"Did you also steal this one?" Raajimera asked.

"Sort of," he responded. "I found it in an abandoned merchant caravan. In my defense, you can't quite pay for the wares if the vendor is nowhere to be found."

The woman grabbed the dress and put it on. It fit her acceptably, although she did feel a little too hot in it.

Zephyr began getting dressed as well.

Raajimera decided to braid her hair. The woman felt like she would overheat if she let her hair down as she usually did. She grabbed one of the ribbons Zephyr had in his trinket box and used it to hold the braid together.

The man passed her a shawl colored like honey.

"Wrap it around your head like a hood," he said. "It will help you blend in better with the imperial women."

Raajimera groaned silently. It seemed to her like there was no escaping from the looming threat of overheating.

Zephyr also wore a shawl, but the one he picked was beige. Browns seemed to fit his personality quite well.

Before they left the hideout, he began concealing her mark once more with his colored rocks.

Raajimera tried not to get sidetracked like she did before. This time she could feel Feng's tiny eyes on her, and she would rather not willingly show her friend how Zephyr made her feel.

The man finished coloring her forehead and put his tools away. Her gaze followed him maneuvering around the clutter of his hideout.

Zephyr began rummaging in one of the bags he brought from his home until he found an item that was familiar to her.

"Why did you bring your horn with you?" she asked, visibly surprised.

"You never know when you might need it," he responded and hid it under his belt, covering it with the lower part of his robe.

When they were finally ready, Zephyr moved towards the door of the hideout.

"Are you going to put supplies on your camel again?" Raajimera asked.

"No," he answered. "I will come back for these things later."

The woman felt a sudden spike in her stomach. She was worried he would go back to the Howling Pass without her after their mission was over.

Zephyr went outside to put a saddle on Bamieh.

Raajimera followed him without a word. She stopped in the door frame and observed him from a distance.

Feng climbed on her shoulder. It looked at her and pressed its cheek against hers.

"Don't worry," the snake said. "We will still be here for you after your mission."

Raajimera sighed.

"I hope you are right," she answered and locked the door behind her.

The sun was already high in the sky, making the air so hot she was not sure if she could stand wearing her disguise.

Raajimera slowly walked towards Zephyr, who had just finished preparing Bamieh for the road. The ugly camel was clearly unbothered, nibbling on a small clump of partially dried-out grass.

Zephyr smiled at her softly and helped her mount the animal; he then sat in front of her. They began moving towards the rock corridors leading to the hideout. She looked back once more, feeling sad about leaving a familiar place behind.

"The capital city is not too far from here," he said. "We will need to leave Bamieh in front of it and travel the remaining distance by foot."

Raajimera nodded. She pressed her face against his back.

They spent most of the time in silence. Both of them were battling their own feelings.

Raajimera couldn't stop worrying about the future. She didn't want him to leave her. The woman felt like she never had this kind of connection with someone before. Never would she realize that in her past life she pushed everyone away as her only goal was power.

Zephyr, on the other hand, was overrun with guilt. He realized that he might have made a mistake bringing her here. The man was not sure whether she could actually succeed, but he did his best not letting her know. He wished he could keep her in the Howling Pass, where he could protect her.

They could finally see the walls surrounding the capital city from a distance.

Zephyr steered the camel towards a small settlement right in front of the east gate. He jumped down and helped Raajimera dismount.

She observed him leading his mount towards a man who owned stables and paying him some money to take care of the camel. The animal handler took Bamieh by the reins, and they both disappeared behind the building.

Raajimera and Zephyr began walking towards the gate. It was partially open.

"Raajimera, there is something I got to tell you before we enter the city," he said.

The woman froze.

"What is it?" she asked.

"I've been observing you all this time, and I connected some dots," he responded. "The way you look and behave reminded me of a rumor I heard. It was about a missing priestess."

Raajimera observed him closely.

"I tried to investigate this clue, but nobody seems to remember neither her name nor appearance," he continued. "They just know they are missing one person. We don't have to do this today. I could take you to the monastery. Maybe someone would finally recognize you and you would find out who you were before the deal with Raa."

Raajimera let out a heavy sigh.

"Zephyr, I really appreciate it, but I feel that I can't go around and chase rumors," she said. "I must serve Raa now. We may not get another chance to sneak up on him. Think about the prisoners too."

Zephyr nodded as he knew she would say that. He wanted to keep her close and protect her with his life. Surely Raa would never agree to that. To the Twilight Mother, she was just a servant, a person to carry out her will in the lands of men. To Zephyr, however, she was just a woman who had been through a lot and still managed to smile. He couldn't care less about her powers; he just wanted to see her thrive.

"I respect your choice," he said.

Raajimera shook her head.

"It's not my choice," she pointed out. "I must do what is right. On my journey I learned that my past would just slow me down. These people need me, Zephyr. I am the only one who can save them from the emperor."

"You are right," he said. "I just wish you didn't have to."

"I know, but if we succeed today, we will not have to hide anymore," she said.

"And if we don't?" he asked. "What if the emperor kills you and uses your powers to destroy the world?"

"Then we will all perish, but at least I will know I died by your side," she answered.

"And Feng called me disturbing," he complained and sighed. "I'm sorry. I just wish I met you before you dedicated your life to Raa."

Raajimera hugged him.

"If your guess is correct and I really was a priestess, I don't think you would be permitted to be alone in one room with me," she said.

He wasn't sure if that was supposed to comfort him, but he laughed softly.

Feng stuck its head from under Raajimera's shawl.

"You two are insufferable!" it yelled. "Can we move already?"

Zephyr looked at the small snake, slightly confused. He pulled back from Raajimera's embrace and fixed his sleeves.

"Before we go inside, I've got to tell you what to do," he said. "Feng, you should stay hidden until we tell you it's safe to go out. Oh, and don't try to talk until then; that would distract us."

Feng was visibly unhappy about it.

"I thought we were friends," the snake complained.

"We are," Zephyr assured the reptile, "but we will need to focus and not raise any suspicion. Normal people don't talk with snakes."

Feng grumpily retreated to its hiding spot.

"And you, Raajimera," Zephyr continued, "don't speak until we reach the palace. You have an accent that could attract unwanted attention."

"But you sound almost the same as me," she pushed back.

"When I speak with you," he corrected her. "In the capital city I use the local accent to blend in. It's your first time in there; it could be difficult for you to sound plausible. It's very important not to alert the guards. The emperor could postpone the wedding and hide if he knows we're here."

Raajimera exhaled heavily.

"Alright, I will be silent," she said.

"Tuck your hair under the shawl too," he told her. "Just in case."

She did as he instructed.

Zephyr offered her his arm, and she took it. They began walking towards the gate.

Raajimera looked at him. She could see he had already gotten into the role. He looked so regal walking with his spine straight like a needle. The hood above his face cast a shadow on his eyes, making him also seem mysterious.

Raajimera felt fairly incompetent seeing him perform like that. With each second passing she could see what he meant.

The gate was tall and made of reinforced wood. It was installed right in the tall beige walls. She didn't expect to feel so intimidated by the entrance alone.

They entered the city, but it wasn't too long until they had been stopped by one of the guards.

"Stop!" he shouted. "Identify yourself."

Raajimera looked at him. She had just noticed that the guard was Serisian like Zephyr, but his complexion suggested he was from another village. He seemed young and inexperienced.

"I'm Zahir," Zephyr lied in the most imperial accent she had ever heard. "I've spent countless moons trying to bring my wife from abroad."

"I need proof," the guard responded.

Zephyr stretched his hand forward, revealing the signet.

"This is all you need to know," he said dryly.

"I'm sorry, sir," the guard apologized. "Although the procedures would require me to also ask your woman..."

"She doesn't speak our language," Zephyr cut short.

"Lucky, I wish mine didn't either," the guard stated. "Just go."

Right before they moved, a lock of hair escaped from under Raajimera's shawl. She quickly tucked it in, but the guard seemed to have noticed.

They proceeded walking down the street. Their shoes danced on the hot cobblestone.

Raajimera was impressed by the architecture of the city. The buildings were tall and colored like the singing dunes behind the Howling Pass. Red imperial banners were flowing down the massive walls, and colorful canopies shielded the merchants from the relentless sun.

The capital city was far more magnificent than she could expect. She could see tall pinnacles pointing up to the sky from a distance. It was a place of many patterns, textures, and colors coexisting with each other peacefully.

It was hard to believe that this city, so beautiful, would be the home for such rotten people.

Zephyr's attention, however, was somewhere else entirely. He saw the man who stopped them at the gate talk to another guard in the corner of his eye. Both men looked at them, gesturing broadly. The gatekeeper returned to his position, but the other guard began following them.

Zephyr squeezed Raajimera's arm and gently pointed in another direction with his chin. They headed towards the bazaar without breaking the beat.

The overwhelming smell of spices made Raajimera feel a little dizzy, but Zephyr didn't slow down.

He led her behind one of the stalls where they could hide between the racks, bending under wares.

Raajimera gently raised her head and saw the guard who was following them. He clearly lost the track of them and went the wrong way, quickly disappearing in the crowd.

Zephyr was about to start moving when he suddenly overheard something interesting.

A group of women stood in front of one of the stalls with fresh olives. They were clearly discussing politics.

"Did you hear about these poor souls from Duskvale?" one of them asked with a squeaky voice. "I heard they interrogated their chieftain."

"Why did they?" another one asked.

"The imperial scouts found traces of magic in the village," the first one answered as she squished an olive in her round fingers. "Our people found a magical flower in a box hidden in his house."

"Oh divinities!" the third one shouted. "A man doing magic?"

The first one paused for a moment, then said, "He claims it was created by a mysterious green-eyed woman of unusual hair color who was marked by one of the gods."

The first lady put the olive away and grabbed another one, examining it closely.

"They have been looking for her for almost a month, but she was nowhere to be found," she finally said. "The emperor just assumed he was lying and put him back in the cell."

"Preposterous," the second woman mumbled.

"I, for one, don't feel comfortable having him around here," the third woman admitted.

"Good thing they are sacrificing them today, then," the second said.

"How can you say that?" the first woman looked at the other two. "They are still just some innocent villagers."

"Clearly not so innocent if their chieftain was captured for doing magic," the second one pointed out. "Imagine what he could have done to us if he reached the capital city."

The woman with a squeaky voice sighed loudly and returned to the olives.

Zephyr looked around and noticed no guards in his sight. He gestured to Raajimera to follow him. They slowly walked until they reached the end of the bazaar.

The great din of the shopping district began fading away as they walked. Raajimera was glad that they didn't have to spend any minute more in that crowded place. The rumors were still echoing in her head.
