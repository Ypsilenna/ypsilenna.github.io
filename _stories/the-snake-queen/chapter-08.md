Raajimera woke up in somebody else's bed. She had a terrible headache that made her feel like her skull cracked into a thousand shards. The woman couldn't figure out what part of the day it was. 

She tried to look around; however, her left side was blocked by a white sheet hanging from the ceiling. Additionally, something big stood in front of the foot of the bed, which effectively obstructed the view of the entire room.

Much to her discomfort, she was covered with what seemed like some sort of a duvet but made of fur. The cursed thing made her too hot; she kicked it away from her. It was a mistake, because the sudden rapid movement brought her pain in places she wasn't even aware she had. 

The burning sensation in her chest didn't let her forget about the wound. She placed her hand on it and noticed that someone had bandaged her. 

The last memories of Duskvale were still haunting her like an awful dream. She couldn't believe that people who were like a family to her would do such a thing. She shook her head, trying to distract herself from the flashbacks, but the image of Seyed's eyes as he held the ritual blade was burning even more than the wound. 

Raajimera knew that the man had two faces, but not to this extent. She didn't expect him to be capable of doing what he did.

Suddenly she heard the door open. The sound startled her, and she jumped up, striking her head against the wooden joist above the bed. The pain was immense, but no greater than her fear. She grabbed the nasty fur cover and buried herself completely under its weight. Raajimera quickly came to regret it, but she couldn't stop panicking. 

With each second, the sound of boots stepping across the stone floor got closer. The woman's heart was pounding in anticipation; she worried it would give her away. 

The white sheet moved. Raajimera tried to take a peek from under the duvet. She saw the source of the noise. 

It was a man who resembled the inhabitants of Duskvale but with a much darker complexion and eyes like amber in warm sunlight. He had curly brown hair tucked under his white hood and the tired face belonged to a middle-aged man who had seen several horrible things too many. 

She couldn't help but feel sympathy for him; however, her recent trauma made her very cautious. 

He gently put his hand on the duvet and asked, "I heard you woke up. How is your injury?"

Raajimera didn't respond. She was harrowed, and she couldn't stop trembling. 

"I'm sorry if I scared you," the man continued. "Would you please let me look at your wound? I brought some medicine for you."

The woman slowly lowered the fur duvet from her head and shyly asked, "Are—are you going to kill me too?"

"What?" The man asked and raised his thick eyebrows. "If I wanted to kill you, I would have done it when you were unconscious instead of dragging you all the way here, given that you wouldn't suffocate first."

Raajimera looked at the floor and said nothing. 

The man sighed. "I need to change your bandages; please, show me your wound," he asked.

Raajimera sat straight on the bed, uncovering her chest. The stranger gently took her bandages off and looked at her wound. 

"It doesn't seem worse," he stated. Not much better either, but at least it's not deteriorating."

The man got up and left for a moment. He returned with a small bowl of water and fresh bandages.

The stranger sat on a small stool next to her and began gently cleaning her injury with a clean cloth dipped in warm water. Once he was done, he put the bowl aside and grabbed a small leather pouch. He opened it and put a small lump of what appeared to be resin on his palm. The clump was colored almost like dried blood.

"What is this?" Raajimera asked. 

"Myrrh, it will help you," he responded and began warming the lump between his hands.

"Is it...is it tasty?" the woman asked with confusion in her voice.

"I will not make you eat it," the stranger responded.

As the resin warmed up, it filled the air with a warm and bitter smell. The man opened his palm. The lump now seemed softer and tackier.

"It might hurt a bit," he said and pressed the chunk against her wound.

Raajimera hissed; her injury was still very fresh. The man clearly downplayed how painful it would be. She hoped it would be over soon, but then the stranger took another clump.

"Patience," he said. "I need to cover the entire wound."

The stranger worked along the wound in an almost meditative way. To Raajimera it felt like an eternity.

Once the man was done, he put the small sack away and wrapped a fresh bandage around her chest.

Raajimera felt relieved, but she couldn't help but ask the question that kept appearing on the back of her head just like an annoying insect in summer.

"Why are you helping me?" she finally asked, visibly struggling to concentrate. "I don't remember seeing you in the village."

"That's because I haven't officially been there since I was seven years old," he responded with a bitter chuckle. 

"You didn't answer the question," Raajimera reminded him.

The man took his things and got up. He looked away and said, "I am helping you because they turned on you as they once did on me."

The mysterious figure put the small leather pouch away on a shelf. 

He paused for a moment, then said, "I was once one of them." 

"I was there more recently than you, and I'm not sure if I really know what happened," Raajimera complained and put her hands on her temples. She felt like there was a halo pressing the top of her head. "How do you know about it? What am I doing here?" she asked.

The man looked at her with a facial expression that explained nothing. He finally said, "I was returning home when I saw you on the ground. The howling wind kept twirling around us, and I couldn't seem to wake you up."

"And who exactly are you?" Raajimera asked him as she made the effort to look around.

"I'm Zephyr of the Howling Pass," he responded.

"That's not a real name," she complained.

"Neither is Raajimera," he answered with a hint of bitterness in his voice. 

The woman gasped. "How do you know my name?" she asked in disbelief.

"Oh, I know more than that." Zephyr said and looked at her indifferently. "One day you appeared in Duskvale out of nowhere. You are a demigod servant of goddess Raa, but you wouldn't talk to the villagers initially, so it took them a while to confirm that. They wanted you to save their village, but no amount of miracles would ever impress them."

Raajimera stared blankly at him.

"I am sorry that I didn't come sooner; I should have known what they were up to," the man mumbled under his breath, just loud enough for her to hear it.

"I don't understand…hold on," she said as a sudden realization sparked on her face. "I heard your name from a traveling merchant. They seemed scared of you in Duskvale. You appear to know about everything that was going on around the village, but you say you haven't been there in years."

"I'm sorry; I know it doesn't seem to have much sense," Zephyr apologized. "The situation there is complicated to me."

"What did you do?" Raajimera asked.

"It's not about what I did." Zephyr sighed and seemed to try to deflect the question. "The dress you're wearing is beautiful. Did they tell you who its previous owner was?" 

The woman was confused by this question and took a second to think about it. She couldn't remember them explaining anything that didn't align with their motives, perhaps not even that. 

"I don't think so," she said after a moment. 

"I knew the previous owner," Zephyr responded gloomily and returned to the stool. 

Raajimera could sense that what she will hear today will shake her perception of the situation. She looked down, trying not to make eye contact with him.

"She was a strong woman," he said. " and the right hand of the previous chieftain. She took care of the villagers, feeding the hungry ones, going out of her way to ensure everyone had enough resources to live a happy and safe life."

"You seem to be looking up to her," Raajimera pointed out.

"I am," Zephyr responded. "Who wouldn't be looking up to their mother?"

The woman didn't respond. She was trying to make sense of the situation, but the pain in her head made it difficult for her to follow. An awkward silence fell between them.

Zephyr wasn't sure how to continue the conversation, so Raajimera used this pause as an opportunity to look around. They were in what appeared to be some sort of a cave, but it was furnished like an actual home. The floors had shelves bending under the weight of various trinkets. There was a hammock hanging on one side and a desk covered with scrolls on the other. Must have been maps. The place looked chaotic but tidy at the same time. It was possible to see harmony between seemingly random objects in a closer look.

Raajimera glanced to her left side and noticed a small silver necklace on the tiny nightstand right next to the bed. She wondered if this man lived alone.

Zephyr noticed her looking at the jewelry piece. He grabbed the item and gently twisted it between his fingers.

"This item used to belong to her as well," he said quietly, as if he wanted to keep it to himself. "You know, I used to live in the village with her. Long time ago."

The man sounded almost melancholic.

Raajimera looked him in the eye. "What happened then?"

"Long story," Zephyr cut short.

He got up and went out of her sight. The man quickly returned with a plain copper kettle in one hand and what appeared to be two small bowls in the other.

"Before I tell you anything, I need to drink this infusion," he explained. "Would you like a cup too?"

Raajimera looked at him suspiciously and asked, "What is it made of?"

"Hop cones… mostly," he responded and smiled at her softly.

"Mostly?" Raajimera inquired. She wasn't sure if she should accept the offer.

"It's not poison; don't worry about it," Zephyr answered. His response sounded almost frivolous.

"It's relaxing, and you seem stressed and in pain. I think it would help you," he explained and poured a small amount of tea into one of the bowls, then handed it to Raajimera.

She hesitantly accepted the drink and carefully took a small sip. Her lips suddenly bent like a hunter's bow.

"What's the matter?" Zephyr asked and raised his eyebrows. He didn't expect this reaction, as he had already forgotten how it felt to drink this brew for the first time.

"It's just a little bitter," Raajimera responded, but a sudden cough muffled her speech. "Why do you drink this stuff?"

"I've had an issue with hand twitching for a long time," he said. "This infusion helps me put them at ease."

The man paused to pour some bitter brew into his cup and put the kettle away onto the nightstand. He sat next to her on the edge of the bed.

"Mister Zephyr—"

"Just Zephyr," the man corrected her and took a sip.

"Right," she said. "You seem to know a lot about me, but I know nothing about you. I've got so many questions."

Zephyr gave a bitter smirk. "I would rather not bore you," he said. "However, I suppose you deserve to know after all you've been through."

Raajimera waited for him to continue, her eyes piercing right through him, like one of the knives she noticed sticking under his belt.

"As I previously mentioned, the dress you're wearing used to belong to my mother," he said.

"Interesting," she remarked. "It was in a building that I thought was the chieftain's treasury."

"Did you think it was his?" Zephyr laughed quietly and continued, "The current chieftain, Abbas, used to be a regular villager, just like the rest. That was until the day when my family disappeared, at least."

"What happened?" Raajimera asked, visibly starting to get into his story.

"The Hazyan Empire aims to unite all nations of Pai-Gorva, this continent. Be it voluntary or not. The Empire, however, only gives these people an illusion of choice. They harass them until they decide to surrender, not enough to wipe them out, but just enough to break their will. Those who request to join the Empire are regarded as its citizens with all rights and obligations. However, the longer the Empire has to fight the nations before they surrender, the harsher they treat them after they ultimately become a part of it. My father was our chieftain. He wanted to bring Duskvale into the Empire to ensure our people would be treated with respect."

Zephyr paused for a short while and looked at his reflection in his cup. 

"Abbas and his family wanted us to remain independent no matter the cost," he finally continued. "He disagreed with my parents so much that one night he made a deal with some imperial soldiers. My family and I were taken as prisoners, and Abbas portrayed us as traitors."

Raajimera glanced at his hands. She could see them shake a little more as he recalled these memories of the past.

"We spent numerous days imprisoned in a dark cell beneath the capital city. Days blended with nights. They would feed us dry bread or sometimes nothing at all. My parents made it their priority to keep me alive, so they would give up their food so that I could eat. We all hoped one day someone would release us, but ultimately, after many cold nights, my parents didn't make it. I still remember the day when the guards dragged their bodies out of the cell."

Zephyr seemed deeply affected by his past; however, he also seemed to have repressed his feelings for so long that he couldn't express them properly anymore. Rough memories left him a broken man on the brink of tears that will never fall.

"I was left in the cell on my own. All alone and scared. It wasn't until one of the guards befriended me. He had recently lost his son. Our grief bonded us, and he did all he could to release me from imprisonment," Zephyr continued, his voice shaking a little. "That man was too good for this world. Thanks to him, I have been accepted as a citizen of the Empire. I was meant to be trained to become an imperial soldier, but after many months, I just couldn't do it anymore. I was overcome with fear and plagued with nightmares. The Emperor wanted to personally oversee my progress. That man…he was terrifying. I still could see his face when I closed my eyes."

Raajimera felt bad for making him talk about his past. She had no idea how badly injured his spirit was.

Zephyr fell silent and took another small sip of his infusion. He took a deep breath and decided to continue.

"The guard who released me could see that there was something very wrong going on with me, yet nothing he did would ease my pain," he said. "In the dead of the night, he helped me flee from the capital city. Unbound by anything, I ran as fast as I could. I ran until I was unable to catch my breath anymore, and my legs were burning. I didn't look back; I was free. That was the night when I became Zephyr. I found my home in the Howling Pass. I couldn't return to Duskvale."

The woman gently put her hand on his. "What did you do afterward?" she asked.

"I became a spy for hire," he answered. "I had to know the affairs both in the Empire and in Duskvale to ensure they would never find me. Turned out I was better than I thought. There were people willing to pay for my services, and I can't say no to a coin."

"Were you on a mission when I arrived in the village?" Raajimera asked, trying to remember that day.

"No," he answered. "You caught my eye one time when I happened to be nearby. The day was turning into the night, and you did something otherworldly next to the well." Zephyr chuckled softly.

"Everyone was too distracted to notice me," he continued. "I decided to investigate the mysterious woman with a curse mark. I should have known what they were up to."

The man sighed.

"What do you mean?" Raajimera asked, clearly surprised. "They did what the old prophecy said they would do."

"That's the whole point," Zephyr said, and he scratched his chin.

Raajimera noticed that his beard actually has two ends, as if he tried to cut a triangle in the middle of it.

"That story was not there back in the day when I lived in the village," he said. "That is a plan, not a prophecy."

Raajimera was perplexed.

"How could they have planned my arrival?" She asked. "Even I didn't know that I would end up there."

Zephyr took a deep breath and answered her question, "By bribing the snake deity for long enough." They didn't ask for you specifically; all they needed was a champion of a spirit beast."

Raajimera looked down.

"I thought they wanted me to save them from the Empire; why would they do this?" She said and touched her wound with her fingertips. "I was told I would be immortal."

Zephyr nervously pulled the earring that he was wearing in his right earlobe. If he had a little more strength in his fingers, he would probably bend the little golden ring.

"Did Raa tell you that?" he asked. "Deities define immortality differently from us. For them it simply means staying young forever. You can still be killed with the right tools. Unfortunately, they possessed them."

"What do you think they wanted me to do, then?" Raajimera demanded an answer.

"I think they wanted to strike the Empire, just on their terms, not yours," he responded.

"Could you explain?" Raajimera requested. She didn't understand him.

Zephyr finished drinking his brew and put the empty cup on the nightstand, right next to the kettle.

"It occurred to me that you've lost your memories," the man stated. "You probably don't remember hearing the tale of the Nightwalker, am I right?"

Raajimera shook her head.

"I'm afraid not," she answered as she tried to finish her nasty infusion as well. The woman tried not to indicate that she hated it.

"The Nightwalker was the fifth celestial animal and the twin spirit of Raa. He was the polar opposite of his sister. A majestic white stag in the night sky, leading the lost and the broken towards the new dawn. He was virtuous and pure. The stag believed that all nations should be liberated, living in peace and harmony; he wanted to see a perfect world.

"To accomplish his objective, he chose the most eminent of warriors to serve as his champion. The only way for gods to have an impact on our world was by blessing someone to become a conduit of their power.

"A great tragedy struck, and the champion of the stag was murdered by a powerful brute, who then realized that killing a champion bestows all their celestial powers upon the killer. However, without the mark of the deity to control him, the assassin could siphon infinite amounts of energy from the stag.

"Armed with raw, uncapped power, the brute lured the Nightwalker out of his hiding and attempted to eliminate him. The warrior hoped that killing a god would make him even stronger. He wanted the entire world to obey his will.

"Unfortunately for him, all the remaining spirit animals combined their powers and destroyed him. What they couldn't foresee, however, was that along with him, the stag's powers vanished as well," Zephyr explained.

"What happened to the Nightwalker?" Raajimera asked.

"He survived, but he was greatly weakened. The stag lost his voice and the shine that made him appear like a star. Nobody ever saw him again," Zephyr answered. "I'm afraid that Abbas intended to recreate these events, destroy Raa, subdue the Empire, and rule the entire Pai-Gorva."

"He was not the one who attempted to kill me," Raajimera opposed.

"No, it was his nephew," the man explained. "Why do you think they shoved him in your face at all times? I overheard them talking about this while scouting the other day."

"His…what?" Raajimera was shocked. "What if the champion were a man? The prophecy said there would be the Snake Queen and the Snake King."

"Abbas has a daughter," Zephyr said. "Aya, you've met her."

Raajimera felt like the entire world just fell on her head.

"She was very formal with him; I didn't know they were a family."

The woman felt betrayed. He used to feel like she could trust Aya. That girl was the first villager to treat her with kindness. In the end it was nothing but an illusion, just like the ones she put into the enchanted flowers.

"That's how both of them were raised," Zephyr said and put his hand on her shoulder. "Their family is very crafty. You didn't deserve it; you are a good person."

Raajimera was on the brink of tears, but unlike Zephyr, she was still able to shed them.

"Nonsense, Raa wouldn't make a deal with me if I were," she cried. "I must have received what I deserved for who I was in the past."

The man seemed to want to comfort her.

"Don't say that," he said. "Your past doesn't matter. You've already paid for it with interest. Trust me, I would like to be your friend. I wouldn't say it otherwise."

Raajimera tried to fight back the tears. She looked at him, her eyes slightly red.

"Thank you…oh no!" she exclaimed. "I forgot about Feng!"

"Who is that?" Zephyr asked, visibly puzzled.

"My snake," Raajimera responded as she searched for her companion erratically. "I must have lost it somewhere."

The woman grabbed her face in panic.

"We will find it," the man said and put his hand on her arm. "I will help you if needed."

Raajimera turned a little calmer upon hearing his words, but she blamed herself for losing Feng.

"I'm worried about it," she admitted. "I think it's my fault that it's gone."

"I know you're worried," he said and got up from the bed.

"Zephyr, what is going to happen now?" Raajimera looked him in the eyes.

He looked back at her and said, "You can stay here until you figure it out."

Zephyr pointed at the hammock hanging in the corner.

"I can sleep there; you keep my bed," he said and paused for a moment. "I will need to leave you alone for several hours now. Do you think you can stay here on your own?"

Raajimera nodded and asked, "Where are you going?"

"I need to trade some items. I will be back late," he answered and pointed at his desk. "If you get hungry, there is a crate of fruit and a jug of water in there."

"Thank you," she said.

"It's nothing," he responded. "If you need more medicine, it's there on the shelf. You could go outside if you feel strong enough already. The door is by the foot of the bed. Just don't go too far; you need rest."

Raajimera nodded once more. 

This time she felt a genuine connection with the person helping her. It meant so much to her; she couldn't even express her gratitude.

Zephyr took his bag with him and left.

Suddenly, once more, she found herself alone in a deafening silence. Raajimera didn't want to be on her own; she missed Feng.

Hours have passed, and she was bored out of her mind. The woman tried to sleep through it, but she woke up after several hours. She didn't know what time it was, but she noticed strong moonlight creeping into the chamber through the hole above the door.

Raajimera decided to get out and get some air. She didn't wear her shoes; she just walked out barefoot. The grains of sand between her toes grounded her to the surrounding nature. She closed her eyes and took a deep breath; the air was fresh.

The woman sat down on the ground and looked up into the stars.

"Oh, Raa. I really need you," she said.

Suddenly the Twilight Mother appeared in the sky.

"Raa?" the woman asked. "What are you doing here at this time? I thought you only showed up during twilight."

"I'm taking care of the night sky for my brother," the goddess responded.

"The stag?" Raajimera asked. "The man who saved me told me about him."

Raa nodded and said, "He speaks the truth."

"Is your brother alive?" Raajimera asked.

The goddess looked at her and responded, "He is, but barely."

There was a certain kind of sorrow in the Raa's voice.

"How is it possible that your brother is a stag and you are a snake?" the woman questioned further. Raajimera felt like her question was inappropriate, but it bothered her enough to ask anyway.

"We are just manifestations of the divine energy. Physical attributes mean nothing to us," the goddess answered.

A moment of silence fell.

"Raa, I seek your guidance," Raajimera admitted. She felt guilty for asking for it.

Zephyr was on his way back home when he saw the spirit of dusk in the sky. He did what he does best; he quietly blended with his surroundings to observe them from hiding. The man noticed Raajimera speaking with the goddess.

"What is it?" Raa questioned.

"It's about the man who rescued me; I am not sure if I should trust him," Raajimera explained. "He told me the story of his life, and I think he really meant it, but I am scared to trust someone after what happened in the village."

"He suffered from their hands just as you did," the goddess answered.

"If you knew what they did to him, why did you send me there?" Raajimera asked in an unintentionally accusatory tone.

"I am a punisher, not a savior," Raa said. "There is a seed of evil within every human being. My role is to chastise the baneful and redeem the worthy. To fully carry out my will, you must rely on the divine judgment and act accordingly. This is your final lesson."

"I don't know how to do what you expect of me," Raajimera admitted.

"Harness your celestial power and bring forth the image of this man's face in your mind. Ask yourself whether he is worthy of redemption from his primal evil. Do so until every fiber of your existence screams out the answer, then act accordingly."

Raajimera closed her eyes. The mark on her head started glowing.

"He is," she said after a moment and opened her eyes.

The sky serpent nodded.

"He is indeed," Raa exclaimed. "Learn to listen to the voice of divine judgment, to trust it and obey its will. Nobody shall ever stand in your way again."

Zephyr smiled softly from hiding. This was the first time he had ever seen the goddess with his own two eyes. Hearing her say that he was worthy meant the world to him.

Raajimera put her hands together, showing her respect to the deity.

"Thank you," she said. "I'm grateful for your wisdom and guidance."

The goddess began vanishing from the sky, but Raajimera remained on the ground. She looked up at the moon and the stars.

Suddenly something poked her leg. She looked down and saw the familiar beady eyes.

"Feng!" she shouted and picked up the reptile from the ground. "You finally found me…"

Raajimera hugged her friend as tightly as she could without damaging its tiny snake body. Her eyes filled with tears. She was not sure if it was happiness or sadness.

"I've finally learned the final lesson. I am about to become a full-fledged demigoddess," she said as she stroked Feng's head with her thumb.

The animal rubbed its cheek against hers. It didn't have to say a word; she was happy to finally hold it in her arms.

Zephyr approached her.

"So, your friend has returned?" He asked and smiled at her warmly.

She nodded quietly, but she was visibly happier.

The man sat next to them and gazed upon the stars with her.

His presence made the night less cold. For the first time since she became a demigoddess, she felt a genuine connection with someone. He was her kindred spirit, and the wisdom of Raa filled her with the certainty that with him by her side everything will be okay.

There will come a day when she will have to face the people who turned on her and make them pay for what they have done. For now, however, all she could think about was the son of the wind, the myriad of stars reflected in his eyes, and the tale of the night sky.
