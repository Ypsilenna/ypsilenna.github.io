The air that morning was as chilly as the heart of the emperor himself. It was true that summer was slowly coming to an end; however, it still felt rather strange to observe such temperatures.

A fresh breeze entered the chamber through the holes in the door, dancing like a performer in front of the royal court.

Raajimera woke up suddenly, shivering under the duvet she hated so much. She turned around and curled up softly in case Zephyr decides to get her out of the bed again.

She felt much better after approximately two weeks of rest. Zephyr took off her bandages the day before. The scar was fully healed and no longer brought her any discomfort. It was rather ugly, yet she refused to attempt to remove it with Raa's magic. She claimed she needed a reminder that she isn't in fact immortal. Raajimera feared that the moment her memento from Duskvale disappeared from her sight, she would grow reckless and end up in peril once more.

The woman suddenly noticed that she must have forgotten to draw the curtain that hung above her bed.

Still quite dizzy from her sleep, she squinted her eyes and noticed that Zephyr got up before her.

The man didn't seem to notice that Raajimera wasn't asleep anymore. He stood with his back towards her, changing his clothes as if the cold didn't bother him at all.

That was the first time Raajimera saw the back of his body. No matter what he did, he rarely exposed his rear side. He wouldn't even let her touch his back. She didn't know why, and it never dawned on her to ask. Now she knew.

Large scars were spread along his spine like creeping vines growing on an old wall of an abandoned building. He seemed to have a dark pattern on his shoulder blades, but it was difficult to make sense of it under all of these marks.

_He must have gotten those when he was imprisoned_, Raajimera thought to herself.

She suddenly felt guilty for staring at him. These scars served as a grim reminder of his troubled past. He didn't have to describe how he got them; their presence already painted a vivid picture in her head.

Raajimera decided to fall back asleep, but her rapid movement alerted Zephyr.

He put his shirt on and adjusted his leather wrist cuffs as he turned around.

"I can see you're not asleep anymore," he stated. "Good morning!"

"Ughhh," she mumbled from under the covers.

Zephyr grabbed a wooden bowl with the intention of making some food for breakfast. He seemed to be in a suspiciously good mood.

"You've heard him!" Feng shouted out of nowhere. "Get out!"

The snake slithered on top of her demanding attention.

"Not you too..." Raajimera said with a voice so miserable as if she were pleading for mercy.

Zephyr dropped the bowl and stared at them with his eyes big and shiny like freshly minted coins.

"Raajimera," he said, "I can understand the snake too!"

Feng looked at him and tilted its head.

"What?" it asked. "But that's impossible! Unless..."

Feng glanced at the woman suspiciously and hissed, "Raajimera, what did you do?!"

She got out of the bed awkwardly and tucked a lock of her hair behind her ear.

"Nothing," she said as she looked away.

Feng looked at her grumpily.

"And how exactly does one teach someone snake speech overnight by doing nothing?" it asked.

Raajimera didn't respond. The situation turned awkward.

Zephyr looked at both of them and said, "You didn't tell me that you could communicate with snakes like **this**." He gestured vaguely. "You said it was your pet."

"Excuse me?! I am not a pet!" Feng shouted, visibly offended. The animal looked at Raajimera and exclaimed, "You are a pet!"

The woman tried to deescalate the situation. "I'm sorry," she said, "I just tried to explain our relationship in a way that a person who can't communicate with snakes would understand!"

"Well, now he does for some reason!" Feng shouted, "You are hiding something, and I'm going to find out what!"

The snake jumped quickly towards the man and began climbing on top of him.

"Hey, it tickles!" Zephyr complained helplessly.

The little reptile sat on the man's shoulder with a determined look on its face. It stuck out its tongue and licked his earlobe.

"Was that necessary?" Zephyr asked.

"No, I just really wanted to do it," Feng responded. The face of the animal looked silly once more.

The man suddenly laughed as heartily as he could. Which wasn't a lot compared to other people, but it was more than he did in years.

"I like this snake," he said, "a delightful little creature."

"I am not little," Feng stated, "I am pocket-sized."

The reptile nodded to give more emphasis to its words.

"Very well," Zephyr responded. "I think I have some clothes with pockets in them in case you want to test that claim."

"He is considerate and has a good taste," Feng shouted. "I don't know what you did, but you chose the right guy; I like him."

Zephyr grabbed the little snake and put it on Raajimera's palms.

Feng looked at her with a very unintelligent expression.

"That was fun, but I have tasted meat, and now I am hungry!" the snake said. "Unhand me so that this predator can catch some prey!"

Raajimera rolled her eyes and put Feng on the floor.

"See you! Don't give him any more powers while I'm gone!" the animal asked and disappeared in the hole under the door.

Suddenly the room fell silent. The only thing she could hear was the sound of Zephyr picking the wooden bowl from the ground.

The woman realized that she was still wearing her nightgown. She grabbed her clothes with the intention of switching them.

"Would you like me to leave so that you can change in peace...?" Zephyr asked while looking for the second wooden bowl. He seemed to have misplaced it somewhere.

Raajimera looked at him and responded, "There's no need. I don't think it matters at this point."

The man found what he was looking for. He carefully stacked the bowls on top of each other and put them on the ground next to him.

"I'm going to make us some breakfast," he said as he opened a container of soaked barley seeds. It was a clay pot with two small handles on both sides. The top was covered with a clay lid.

"Oh, but I'm not hungry yet," Raajimera responded while putting her earrings back in her ears. She felt a little guilty for saying that.

"Don't worry, it takes a little longer to cook it," Zephyr assured her.

He put the bowls on top of the container and attempted to carry them outside all on his own.

"Hold on, let me help you!" Raajimera shouted and snatched the bowls from the stack he was holding.

"Thank you, but I've been doing this alone for years. I can handle it," he said.

"Yes, but you are not alone anymore," she reminded him. "I want to be a part of your life too, please include me."

"Fine," he responded and gestured at the door.

Raajimera opened the door for him and followed him outside.

The man put the container of barley seeds on the ground and knelt in front of a small structure next to the entrance. It was a fireplace with a copper pot suspended above it on a wooden construction.

Zephyr got up and returned to his house to bring some utensils and firewood outside. He knelt again and put some wood under the pot. The man pulled a firelight kit from his pocket and began starting the flame.

Raajimera looked around. She usually went out at night fearing someone would spot her. Now she could see the Howling Pass clearly in the sunlight. The mountains surrounding this place were almost orange, covered sparsely with the only vegetation that could survive the relentless sun.

The woman glanced at Zephyr's house and noticed how well the entrance blended with the surrounding rocks. It looked as if the entire place were a part of the mountain.

"Is this the only road that leads to Duskvale?" she asked, attempting to remember how she reached it in the first place.

"No," he responded. "There are other ways to reach it. People generally avoid this path because it terrifies them."

"But why?" Raajimera kept asking. Her curiosity could never be satiated.

"I will explain; let me start cooking this first," Zephyr requested.

The woman sat on a rock by the entrance and waited. She curiously observed Zephyr.

The man put the barley seeds in the copper pot and waited for the water to start boiling. He stirred them once and put the lid on top.

"It can stay like this for now," Zephyr said. "I will show you something now; follow me."

He entered the house and took a strange-looking horn off his shelf. It looked like a piece of wood with animals carved on it. It was embellished with metallic parts and had a leather strap on top. Zephyr attached the horn to his belt and led her outside.

"There is a small hidden road nearby that leads to an abandoned watchtower," he said. "It was already there when I moved in. I don't know who built it, but it's very useful to get a better view of the area."

Raajimera followed him.

The man led her in front of a crudely carved set of stairs in the mountainside. There was a worn-out rope on the left side of it, possibly installed in there as a safety measure, although currently it would be safer to simply ignore its existence.

Raajimera clumsily began climbing the stairs behind him. Zephyr turned around and looked at her.

"Be careful in this part; I've just finished putting you back together," he said. "Grab my hand; it will be easier for you."

The woman looked at him. He extended his hand towards her, and she gently grabbed it.

After some struggle, they finally reached the watchtower. It was a small wooden construction approximately as secure as the rope along the stairs.

Zephyr helped her get inside and pointed to the horizon.

"Do you see that desert over there?" he asked.

"Yes, I do," she responded, squinting her eyes.

"The dunes of that desert are truly special and a miracle of nature," Zephyr stated.

"How so?" she asked. "I thought they were just... dunes."

"They sing," he answered. "When the sand moves, it makes an eerie sound that then travels this way and echoes against the walls of the pass. The travelers find this sound deeply unsettling, almost haunted."

"Is that why it's called Howling Pass?" Raajimera asked.

Zephyr nodded. "It is. Most people believe this place is cursed partially because of that. The other reason is that they believe there are fearsome beasts making their home in the pass. I would lie if I said I didn't have my hand in this particular tale."

"But I haven't seen any beasts in here," Raajimera complained. "Not only the fearsome ones; I don't recall any sort of beasts around here."

Zephyr smiled softly and said, "Let me show you something."

He pointed at a person walking in the visible distance. Far too close for comfort. Zephyr removed the horn from his belt, wiped it softly with his fingertips to get rid of the dust, and then blew.

A disturbing sound echoed across the pass. It sounded partially like a lion's roar and partially like the voices of a thousand damned souls cast into the depths of the underworld.

The sound made Raajimera's skin crawl. She looked at the stranger down below and saw him flee in terror. The poor man was screaming like a little child.

Zephyr's eyes followed him for a moment until the stranger could no longer be seen. He then reattached the horn to his belt.

"So these people are terrified of something fearsome and sinister, but it was just you all along?" Raajimera looked at him and asked.

"Are you saying I'm not fearsome and sinister?" Zephyr responded with a question. "I am deeply wounded by your statement."

He theatrically put his hand on his chest to let her know that he was just joking.

After spending some time with him, Raajimera already learned to read the undertones of his otherwise flat way of speaking. She noticed the joke and laughed softly.

"No, towards me you aren't," she responded and moved closer to him. She gave him a gentle but tender kiss.

"Raajimera, please, not here," he said and softly pushed her back. His cheeks turned a little rosy.

"Is it because someone could see us here?" she asked with a hint of disappointment and confusion in her voice.

Zephyr shook his head and responded, "No, it's because this construction is probably older than the empire itself, and as much as departing from this life with an attractive demigoddess on top of me sounds tempting, I still have some unfinished business."

"Oh," Raajimera blabbered.

Zephyr looked her in the eye, gently stroked her cheek with his thumb, and led her back down.

The man returned to his cooking pot. He stirred the barley seeds and added some salt, apple cider, and cardamom. The spices smelled quite good.

Zephyr fetched some dried apricots from his house and cut them into pieces as he waited for the porridge to boil. When the dish was almost ready, he put the fruits inside and mixed them with honey.

The man put the porridge into the wooden bowls and topped it with some milk. They sat down on a blanket and ate the dish together.

"Where do you get all these spices from?" Raajimera asked as she took a spoonful of the porridge. It was warm, sweet, and had a fruity flavor from the apricots. It was thick, but just the right amount.

"I trade a lot; you have no idea what kind of things people are willing to give you in exchange for secrets," he responded.

"Isn't that dangerous to put yourself out there?" she inquired. "Your looks are quite distinct, and most of the scouts I've seen were human."

"Oh, you mean because I'm a Serisian?" he questioned and looked at her.

"What is that?" she asked, confused.

"People of villages like Duskvale are Serisian," he explained. "That's how people call my kind. There are more of us in other villages to the north, although we are scattered all across the empire. Sticking together has never been a Serisian trait."

"Oh, yes, that's what I meant, actually," Raajimera answered, visibly embarrassed. "Your eyes look so unique, and every person of your kind I met had a different pattern on their skin. Wouldn't it make it easier to tell you apart?"

"Perhaps," he responded. "There is a spot not too far from here where you can extract colored rocks. I sometimes use them to cover my marks or color my hair. Outfits also do a lot to confuse strangers."

"You are so crafty, Zephyr," Raajimera stated. "I am honored to stay in your home and see the real you."

Zephyr smiled softly. "I am honored that you saw the real me and didn't end up disappointed. I mean, look at you. You are a powerful demigoddess destined for greatness; I am just a broken person."

These words upset Raajimera a little.

"You were not in charge of what happened to you," she said. "You just had to learn how to navigate your new life... like I did."

Zephyr nodded gently.

"Perhaps this is why I could deceive everyone else, yet I can't deceive you," he mumbled under his breath.

"I wish you didn't have to go through all of that, but for what it's worth, I think you are wonderful," Raajimera said as she finished her bowl of porridge, "and so is this dish. You are a great cook, Zephyr."

"I do what I can," he responded and finished his portion as well.

He took their bowls and cleaned them outside with a little water.

"Tell me, Raajimera," Zephyr said, "would you like to see my camel?"

"Your... what?" she asked, unsure.

"My camel... a mount?" he explained, gesturing broadly. "You know, an animal?" "

"Oh, the animal, right..." she said. "I didn't realize you had one; for some reason I just thought you went everywhere by foot."

"I'm not sure if I should ask what you think I meant, and I think I am better off not knowing," he stated. "Do you want to see it, then?"

"Of course," Raajimera assured him. "That was exactly what I thought you meant. Lead me to it."

Zephyr looked at her suspiciously and then gestured at her to follow him.

In a short while they arrived at a little hidden corral. The man whistled, and the animal peeked from inside its shelter. The camel approached him with overwhelming trust in its eyes.

"Bamieh!" Zephyr exclaimed. "Good morning, friend!"

The man scratched his furry companion across its neck and said, "Did you miss me, boy?"

Zephyr gave his animal some food. The gentle interaction between him and the camel hinted how much mutual trust there was between them.

Raajimera felt as if something incredibly stupid were trying to course through her body and leave through her mouth. Against her better judgment, she blurted out, "Zephyr, no offense, but this is possibly the ugliest camel I could have imagined..."

The man laughed softly and said, "None taken. This is actually why I adopted him. He reminds me of myself."

"What?" Raajimera exclaimed. "But you're not ugly!"

"Say what you want, but ever since you appeared here, I am no longer the most beautiful person in my house," he said.

Raajimera scratched her head and responded, "Huh? You didn't strike me as a flirty type when I first met you..."

Zephyr looked at her with a disproportionally serious facial expression and said, "And you didn't strike me as the kind of person who would react the way you did when I last took your bandages off. You really took me off guard, but I admit I really admire your strength."

Raajimera was flushed.

"Don't say this out loud!" she protested.

"Why? Because someone could hear me?" he asked and pointed at his belt. "I still have my horn with me if this is what you're worried about."

She didn't know how to respond, so she just fell silent.

"Would you like to go for a ride?" Zephyr broke the silence.

Raajimera nodded, still feeling embarrassed from their prior interaction.

The man walked towards the little shed that was right next to the corral. He rummaged in it for a moment and then pulled out a shawl.

"Take this," he said, handing Raajimera the piece of cloth. "It smells like a camel, but at least it will protect you from the wind and sand."

He didn't lie. The shawl really did smell like an animal, but Raajimera wore it one way or another.

Zephyr took one of his ornate rider blankets off the wall and placed it on the camel's back. He then proceeded to attach the saddle as well. The man looked at Raajimera and noticed her blank stare.

"Listen," he said, "it had been years since the last time someone was here with me like this. I spent so much time pretending that I was someone else that I forgot how to be myself. Tell me if I say or do something that bothers you."

"I will," she responded and approached the camel.

The animal was quite big and had large yellow teeth sticking out of its lower jaw. She hoped the ride would be more pleasant than its appearance.

Zephyr led the animal out of the corral and helped Raajimera sit on its back. She was scared, but the experience was better than she thought it would be.

The man sat right behind her, and their journey commenced. Raajimera looked at their surroundings curiously. She didn't expect the ride would be so fun. Zephyr held her close to make sure she wouldn't fall. She felt so safe in his arms.

They suddenly noticed something disturbing from the distance.

"Isn't that Duskvale?" Raajimera asked.

"It is," he responded, "but what happened to it?"

They approached the village cautiously. Raajimera noticed that the protective barrier she created was down. Half of the buildings were demolished, while the other half were burned down.

Zephyr got down from his camel's back and helped Raajimera dismount as well. The two silently roamed around the ruins of what was previously a lively village. They tried to find some survivors, but to no avail.

Raajimera knelt down and noticed the shards of her flowers mixed with the sand. She followed their trail straight into the well. She looked inside but saw nothing.

Zephyr tried searching the buildings. He noticed the invaders destroyed the valuable items inside rather than taking them. He made a step, and something made a sound under his shoe. He picked the item up and investigated it closely. He was sure he had a clue in his hand.

The man approached Raajimera to tell her about his discovery, but he saw her standing still in front of the well.

He approached her from behind and gently touched her arms.

"What are you looking at?" he asked, trying to trace her sight.

She silently pointed at the ground.

The sand was visibly disturbed. It showed signs of struggle. She figured out the villagers tried to oppose the raiders as much as they could given their state when she left them. The thing that drew her attention, however, was a small wooden toy with a crack, possibly from being hit by a pike.

Raajimera picked the object up and rotated it in her fingers. It was a well-loved wooden crocodile.

"This toy belonged to Adel, a young boy I protected once," she said. "His mother was so overjoyed to have him back that she couldn't stop crying."

Zephyr nodded quietly.

"Raajimera, I found something in the sand," he said and showed her the object. "I think I know who took him."

She glanced at the item. It looked like a small golden button with the symbol of the empire.

"What is it?" she asked.

"It's a cufflink," he responded. "This type is worn by the imperial military units."

"So they were taken there," she said. "But why now?"

Zephyr thought about it for a moment; a sudden realization appeared in his mind.

"The emperor's wedding..." he mumbled. "But that can only mean one thing. They aren't **guests**."

Raajimera looked at Zephyr.

"Why would he need the villagers at his wedding?" she asked, trying to piece these two bits of information together.

"Raajimera, you don't understand," Zephyr said. "He doesn't need people **at** his wedding; he needs them **for** his wedding. I don't think anyone ever told you what kind of a person he really is."

"You said he was cruel and terrifying," Raajimera recalled.

"He is, but that's not all," Zephyr explained. "He allowed the Ring of the Diurnal Cycle to operate in the empire to give people an illusion of choice, but he forces his subjects to worship the Gods of the Underworld."

The woman shook her head.

"I don't know what it means," she admitted. "I've never heard of them before."

"The emperor wanted to be a champion like you, but beings of the underworld only accept blood sacrifice," Zephyr said. "They never fully blessed him, but they assisted him enough to make him almost a demigod. He dabbled in blood magic, so nobody dared to oppose him."

"So you think he wanted to sacrifice an entire village for the gods of the underworld to bless his new marriage?" Raajimera asked.

"Exactly. The wedding is in about two weeks," Zephyr informed her. "There is still time if you want to go there and rescue them... or the children at least."

Raajimera looked at the toy on her palm. Her fingers wrapped all around it, squeezing the object so hard that she nearly broke it for good. Her hands were shaking.

"It's not enough to save the children," she said through clenched teeth. "Zephyr, as long as that man breathes, none of us will be safe. Not you, not me, not this or any other village. I think I see now why Raa sent me here."

Zephyr didn't respond. He just quietly listened to her.

"I knew I was sent here for a reason," she said. "All this time I thought I was meant to save Duskvale. Their betrayal made me question my purpose, but now I see it clear as the day."

Raajimera looked at Zephyr.

"I got to get rid of the emperor once and for all," she said. "I have to take his place and ensure that no other child will ever suffer as you did. Such is the will or Raa."

Zephyr looked at her face. For a split second he felt as if he were looking at Raa herself.

"Whatever you plan to do, I pledge my help," he said as he put his hand on his heart.

"I will need your subtlety and knowledge," Raajimera stated. "I believe our mission has the highest chance of success if we sneak into the capital city and strike from the shadows."

"You are right," Zephyr agreed. "He is powerful, and facing him could raise the stakes that are already high."

The woman looked at him and said, "You're right. If he killed me, he would claim the power of Raa and doom this world."

"That too," Zephyr answered, "but my first thought was that I couldn't bear losing you."

Her eyes filled with tears. She discarded the toy she was holding and hugged him.

He gently kissed her forehead right next to her hairline and led her back to the camel. They mounted up and headed home.

Her thoughts were racing. For the first time since the beginning of her journey she could see her objective clearly, yet she didn't know if she had what it takes to fulfill it. All she was sure of was that she was the only person in this world who could end the cycle of violence that pestered the empire for decades.

Was this even what she wanted in her past life? That question will forever remain unanswered.
