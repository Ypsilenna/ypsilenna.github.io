Life in the distant village of Duskvale was far from perfect, yet its inhabitants seemed to make the most of it given their situation. The adult part of the population had their hands full of errands. Oftentimes this would pose a problem, as their children were usually extra playful. 

The imperial threat seemed to have subsided; most of the villagers grew listless and dropped their guard. Others, however, were more vigilant than usual. The silence before the storm spoke louder to them than a thousand war horns.

Raajimera was having a stroll around the settlement when one woman stopped her, asking for her aid. 

"Snake Queen?" the lady said as she tried to get her attention. "Excuse me, but I've got a favor to ask. I am looking for my son, Adel. I—I think he left the settlement, but nobody would treat me seriously," she stuttered.

Raajimera looked at her with a smile and said, "I know your son; don't worry! I will bring him back to you."

The village woman tried to fall to her knees and kiss the Snake Queen's feet, but Raajimera stopped her. She appreciated the gesture, but she found it unnecessary.

Adel was a troublemaker; wherever he went, he probably brought the rest of the kids with him. Raajimera decided to begin her investigation. 

After two hours of fruitless searching, she decided to sit down and think. That's when she heard the voice of one of the children on the outskirts of the village. It sounded suspicious, though. She carefully approached and hid behind a boulder, peeking from behind. 

Two strange men towered above the group of terrified children. The shape of their bodies reminded her of two massive wardrobes. Both of them wore their red uniforms. There was a good chance they were scouts from the Hazyan Empire.

That was the first time she had ever seen someone from there. Despite all the times villagers mentioned it, she almost thought the empire was just a legend. She had to come up with a plan; these men looked as terrifying as she had imagined them.

All of a sudden, she noticed someone approaching them. It was Seyed, seemingly trying to fight them off. The man obviously wanted to be the hero, but these two would easily fold him like an ornate blanket. 

Raajimera was worried that Seyed's reckless actions would put the children in danger. The imperial scouts were armed, after all.

She poked her pocket. "Feng? Would you help me here?" she asked.

The snake yawned and slithered to her shoulder, looking at the group of people in the distance.

"Yes, those are definitely people," Feng said.

"I don't need you to identify them!" Raajimera responded.

"What would you like me to do, then?" the animal asked. "I could punch them, but you would need to lend me a **hand.**"

"No!" Raajimera moaned, frustrated. "Tell me how to get rid of these two men who threaten the children!" 

"Just for the sake of clarity," Feng hissed and looked at her, "you meant the two people in red, right?"

"Why is that even a question?" Raajimera asked and looked at the snake with disbelief.

"If you don't understand the sophisticated art of jokes, just say so," Feng responded. The animal wanted to make it obvious that it was rolling its eyes, so it dramatically rolled the whole head as well. "This situation calls for an illusion."

The woman waited for the snake to continue. 

"Can you remember what the air looks like above a hot road?" Feng asked.

"I am not sure how that question is relevant, but yes." Raajimera said and exhaled heavily, clearly anticipating yet another **joke**.

"You can use any energy—either from your body or from your surroundings—to dissolve it into such a shape with your willpower," Feng explained.  
The animal tried to be as precise as possible. "You can then visualize an object and bind it to the energy to shape it into whatever it is you desire," it said.

"That makes sense, I think," Raajimera said as she tried to process the new information.

"Do you remember that trader who passed through the village several days ago?" the snake questioned.

The woman nodded. "Yes, the man with a tiger in a cage," she recalled.

"Precisely," Feng said and nodded. "You could shape your energy into a tiger or two. Maybe three?"

In a flash, three scary-looking tigers jumped from behind the boulder and walked towards the scouts. Their steps made no sound, so Seyed couldn't see or hear them. His back was facing the illusions.

The images of tigers were truly menacing and realistic. They seemed as if they were on a hunt. The illusions were almost perfect in every way.

Suddenly, the scouts noticed them and backed away. 

Raajimera shattered the illusions and joined the children. 

Seyed's eyes shifted towards her. "Oh, it's you, milady!" he shouted with pride in his voice. "Have you seen how these imperial bastards ran away at my sight?"

Raajimera nodded; she didn't have the heart to tell him what really happened.

"They didn't stand a chance!" the man continued bragging like a little boy. It was a little annoying, but the only thing that mattered for Raajimera was that the children were safe at last. 

She turned around to try to return the kids to the village, but suddenly Seyed grabbed her hand and kissed it. She was perplexed.

"What do you think you're doing?" Raajimera asked, making him understand that she wasn't completely fine with what was happening. 

"My apologies," he said, letting go of her hand but continuing to talk, "but I promise to protect you forever if you choose me as your king. Just as I did today, milady."

Raajimera was visibly embarrassed. She felt that the man was making it unnecessary and too complicated. However, she would have sworn that for a split second something changed in his expression. It was then replaced by his regular smile, which made Raajimera think she had probably just imagined it.

"The only thing I want from you presently is helping me get the children to safety," the woman said. She looked at them and asked, "Are you alright?"

The children seemed fine; they were just scared. One of them nodded and rubbed his eyes. The little boy seemed to try his best to appear brave.

Raajimera and Seyed escorted them back home. The villagers rejoiced. It was confusing. The children were crying, and their mothers as well. Everyone was hugging. The entire village was too loud, but currently it was a good thing.

Raajimera saw the chieftain and decided to talk to him. As she approached him, Seyed appeared seemingly out of thin air and joined them.

"There is something you should know—" she tried to start a conversation, but Seyed interrupted her.

"I spotted two scouts from the Empire," he said.

"I was about to—" she kept trying to finish her sentence, but this man was relentless.

"I scared them away, but it is only a matter of time until they are back and in greater numbers," Seyed said. He kept talking as if somebody paid him for every word that went through his brain-mouth junction. 

Raajimera began feeling annoyed by this man. She wanted to punch him in the face.

The chieftain just said, "We should organize an emergency meeting and discuss it with everyone"; then he left, and Seyed disappeared with him.

"Incredible," Raajimera said to herself in disbelief. 

Feng slithered out of her pocket and said, "It's still not too late to create another illusion. What do you think Seyed fears?"

The woman didn't want to engage in this conversation, as she knew it was merely a setup for yet another joke. 

The snake continued, "Maybe water? A spirit telling him he will lose his muscles?"

"Oh, shut it already." Raajimera was still upset and didn't want to hear anything more.

Feng understood and fell silent. The animal decided to retreat to the safety of her hidden pocket.

All the villagers gathered in a stone circle at the village's edge. The men built a bonfire and sat around it. Some of them began grilling small pieces of food above it as they waited. 

"I know that you don't want to listen to me at the moment," Feng started, "but can you sit as close to the fire as possible?"

"I guess," she responded, "what for, though?"

"I will tell you later; just join them now." Feng retreated to her pocket.

Raajimera sat on a stone that was very close to the fire. She felt the heat on her face, but she enjoyed seeing the warm light of the flames. 

One of the athletes approached her and had a quick chat with her. He seemed like a swell person. Raajimera realized that she never really talked to any athlete besides Seyed, but they were actually great. 

The remaining athletes joined her; one of them even brought her something to drink. It was some sort of tea, but it had an interesting floral flavor. The woman enjoyed it a lot.

The chieftain appeared and began giving his speech. It was very long, and Raajimera couldn't remember a word from it. All she knew was that it was about the situation on the outskirts of the village. The chieftain urged his people to stay vigilant, which didn't surprise her. 

The athletes kept trying to get her attention, and she enjoyed having some meaningless casual chat with someone for once. It was a breath of fresh air for her.

Seyed was watching her from the opposite side of the stone circle. He seemed jealous, but he would rather not get up and join them. Perhaps it would seem too obvious if he did?

Feng slithered up Raajimera's shoulder and whispered in her ear, "Can you create more crystal flowers like the ones you created at the shrine?"

"I can. The question is… will I?" she responded.

Feng didn't enjoy not being the one to make a joke for once. The animal looked at her, frustrated.

"Hey, stop it!" Feng shouted. "I am serious."

"Well, well, look how the tables have turned," Raajimera responded. "Alright, I will do it."

She got up and knelt in front of the bonfire. 

The people kept discussing the safety of the village. She was listening, but only partially. Raajimera was captivated by her craft, and maybe every third word passed through her ears. Even fewer stopped within her skull on their way out through the other ear.

She heard something about the east road. The inhabitants of Duskvale sounded upset, probably rightfully so. Her eyes perked up briefly when some of them said, "They are coming," followed with "three days."

Raajimera looked up for a moment. The chieftain started shouting at someone. She made another flower; it was much easier to distract herself with her art. It wasn't like they would let her participate in the exchange anyway. Despite everything, she still felt like an outsider.

She thought she heard something about soldiers, but the words quickly drowned somewhere behind her as she entered a trance. The woman just wanted to create more and more. Every next flower was even more beautiful than the previous one. Raajimera thought she found the perfect technique for conjuring them. 

Suddenly something pulled her out of the reverie, and she jumped in place, dropping one of the flowers. She looked at the miserable glass shards on the ground. It was such a sad way to go.

"I'm sorry! Did I scare you?" a familiar voice asked.

She looked up. It was Seyed, who was hanging above her with a goofy smile. 

He waited for no answer; he simply continued, "I just came here to compliment the, uh, things you crafted. I think they are very beautiful!" 

Raajimera bent her lips in a quite unconvincing smile. "Thanks, but I am quite busy right now. Shouldn't you—" she paused and looked around. It had just struck her that the meeting was over.

"Oh," she mumbled to herself.

Seyed laughed. "Now I can see it," he said. "I'll take care of my business, then. See you later!" He waved at her and left. 

Feng lifted its snout and looked at Raajimera. The look on her face was like an enigma; it was so difficult to crack.

"What are you thinking about?" the animal asked curiously.

"Sometimes I wish I could mind control him," she confessed. "I would like to know what really is on his mind."

"Why would you say that?" Feng was confused. 

Raajimera looked at her companion and let out a sigh. "There is something strange about him; I just can't quite tell what," she said, then stopped and scratched her head. "I mean, he is handsome and appears to be amicable, but whenever he speaks, something in my mind begins to scream. I'm not sure if you get my point."

"Hmmm…" Feng pondered. "Thus, you both like and dislike him."

"Exactly!" Raajimera exclaimed. She tried to keep her feelings in check. "It's so confusing. He gives me no reason to doubt him, but something in his behavior stings me like a splinter."

"I don't know what to tell you," the snake admitted, "but if he makes you feel unsafe, just remember that this place is full of sand, and he has two clean eyes with a striking lack of dust in them."

Raajimera laughed quietly. "Anyway, would you mind telling me why you asked me for the glass flowers? I made so many, but I don't know if it's enough for whatever you had in your mind."

Feng looked at the pile of the figurines she had made. "No, that's too many, but I admire your dedication."

"You should have just told me how many you wanted," Raajimera said, pouting.

The snake slithered towards the flowers. The animal tilted its head and said, "I wanted to show you how to enchant them to host your illusions."

"Why would I do that?" Raajimera asked.

"You could bind them to your crystals, and they would persist until the flower is destroyed." Feng hissed and continued, "You could bind most of your demigod abilities to them to create decoys, protection spells, or just plain decorations."

"Fascinating," Raajimera whispered and grabbed one of her glass flowers to take a closer look. "Does that mean I could create a quicksand trap with one of these?"

Feng nodded. "Precisely," it said. "You could create some enchantments that would aid this village; let me explain." The animal jumped down from her lap to the ground and landed with a soft thud. 

Raajimera waited for her scaly friend to finish what it started saying.

The snake opened its mouth widely, trying to grasp air, thinking it would make it seem more sophisticated, while in reality it just looked like a big, goofy mouth with a tail and nothing in between. "Create an illusion like you did the last time," Feng said finally. "Before you finish casting it, focus on the crystal and redirect your energy into it."

After several unsuccessful attempts and a couple of swear words, Raajimera decided to start with a smaller illusion. She finally succeeded at her task; she just found it quite underwhelming. 

The woman looked at her enchanted crystal, and two small beady eyes of a cricket looked back at her from it. 

"I guess that's all I am capable of currently," she said with a hint of disappointment in her voice. "How do I make it go outside the flower now that it's inside?" she asked. "If I even can do it, that is."

"Simply put it on the ground," the animal responded. Feng would be shrugging if it had arms to do so. 

Raajimera carefully placed her crystal on the ground. After several seconds, a small cricket hopped on the surrounding ground. 

Seeing the spell work made Raajimera so happy. She quickly got up, accidentally kicking a rock right into her flower and shattering her illusion.

"Nooooo!" she shouted, "my creation!" The woman felt like she was punched in the gut. 

"Just make another one, now that you know how to do it," Feng advised. It didn't know why its friend reacted the way she did.

"But this one was special! It was the first one," Raajimera complained. "I wanted to keep it and treasure it…"

"Listen, don't hang on to things so much," the snake said. "This is what prevents us from making progress. It was there; now it isn't, but what's important is that now you can do it."

The woman looked at the snake. "You're right," she admitted. "Now that illusion is in the past, and I need to focus on here and now to hone my skills."

Feng was happy that she remembered its words. 

"Although it's so difficult to forget about it," she said quietly, as if she didn't want her companion to hear it.

"That's the difficult part of being able to remember things," Feng stated. "The past isn't physically here anymore, but the emotional weight it has makes it difficult for us to move on."

Raajimera nodded bitterly and said, "I think Raa did me a favor, then." 

"In a way, I think she did," Feng said. "Why don't you pick me up and have a stroll around the village to clear your head?"

The woman agreed. She distributed the remaining flowers evenly between her two hidden pockets, extinguished the dying bonfire, and grabbed the snake gently. 

They wandered across the shabby roads of the village. The little glass flowers were buzzing with each step she took.

The sun was high in the sky, but the settlement seemed empty. _They are probably napping_, she thought to herself. 

The air was much hotter now than it was earlier that day. Raajimera found it much less pleasant than the flames of the bonfire she sat by this morning, but she tried not to let it get to her.

The woman sat on a barrel in the shade of one of the buildings. She saw the surrounding areas. There was another village on the horizon, some hills, and mountains. Raajimera saw a gap between them and began wondering what was in there. She suddenly had a memory of seeing a map in the chieftain's place. 

Feng tried to figure out what she was looking at. "What is it that caught your attention?"

"That break between the mountains, I think they called it Howling Pass," she responded. "I wonder what's in there. Do you think the imperial soldiers came from that direction?"

"I don't know," Feng responded. "I'm just a snake."

Raajimera gently stroked the animal's cheek and smiled. 

She couldn't help but observe the horizon. She had a feeling that something or someone was coming, watching, observing… She hoped she could protect her people from whatever that was. To treat them the way they deserve.

Regardless of what it meant.
